Case 35-2003 — A 75-Year-Old Man with a Cystic Lesion of the Pancreas
Andrew L. Warshaw, M.D., William R. Brugge, M.D., Kent B. Lewandrowski, M.D., and Martha B. Pitman, M.D.

Presentation of Case

A 75-year-old man was evaluated in the clinic because of a cystic lesion of the pancreas.

For several years he had had gastroesophageal reflux, which was alleviated by pantoprazole. He continued to have intermittent, mild, vague upper abdominal pain with possible early satiety. The pain did not radiate to the back. He had not lost weight. An ultrasonographic examination performed at another hospital four months before admission showed a hyperechoic mass, 2.9 cm in diameter, in the right hepatic lobe that was eventually determined to be a hemangioma, as well as multiple hepatic cysts. Computed tomographic (CT) scanning of the abdomen performed with the use of contrast material at the same hospital revealed a cystic lesion in the pancreatic duct at the junction of the body and tail of the pancreas, with evidence of slight dilatation of the duct, and a fluid-filled structure, 2 cm in diameter, that was contiguous with the duct. The duct was beaded and dilated from the head to the tail. The biliary tract was unremarkable. The results of liver-function tests performed at that hospital were normal, but the iron saturation was 53 percent. He was referred to this hospital for further evaluation.

The patient was a retired engineer. He had a history of hypertension that was well controlled with doxazosin and hydrochlorothiazide. He had consumed four or five alcoholic beverages daily until a few years before the current evaluation and thereafter consumed one drink daily. He did not smoke.

The temperature was 35.8°C, the pulse 75 beats per minute, and the respiratory rate 20 breaths per minute. The blood pressure was 125/70 mm Hg. On physical examination, the patient appeared well. No rash or lymphadenopathy was found, and the lungs, heart, abdomen, and arms and legs were normal. Neurologic examination revealed no abnormalities.

The urine was normal. The hematocrit was 38.3 percent, the white-cell count 6400 per cubic millimeter, the platelet count 359,000 per cubic millimeter, and the mean corpuscular volume 92 µm3. The prothrombin and partial-thromboplastin times were normal. The levels of urea nitrogen, creatinine, glucose, conjugated and total bilirubin, electrolytes, aspartate aminotransferase, amylase, and alkaline phosphatase were normal. Chest radiographs showed a questionable, ill-defined nodular opacity in the superior part of the right hilar region; the thoracic aorta was tortuous.

Differential Diagnosis

Dr. Daniel L. Overdeck (Radiology): CT scanning of the abdomen performed at the other hospital showed atrophy of the entire pancreas and diffuse dilatation of the pancreatic duct, which measured approximately 1 cm in diameter. There was no evidence of calcification within the surrounding pancreatic tissue. No mass was seen in the head of the pancreas. Immediately superior to the pancreatic tail was a mass, approximately 2 cm in diameter, that showed homogeneous low attenuation similar to that in the pancreatic duct, suggesting communication between the mass and the duct. Multiple cysts and a hemangioma were seen in the liver. There was no evidence of dilatation of the biliary duct or enlargement of the peripancreatic lymph nodes.

Dr. William R. Brugge: This patient's initial tests consisted of an endoscopic evaluation of the ampulla of Vater, endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography, and an endoscopic ultrasound study.

The endoscopic examination of the ampulla showed that it was slightly enlarged and that mucin was extruding through the orifice (Figure 1). An endoscopic ultrasound examination was performed to evaluate the pancreas. A linear endoscope was placed in the stomach, and the body of the pancreas was examined. The main pancreatic duct was diffusely dilated to a diameter of 3 mm (Figure 2A); the normal diameter of the duct is 1 to 2 mm. The pancreatic parenchyma was normal. A smooth-walled, round, anechoic cystic lesion within the pancreatic parenchyma was visible and appeared to communicate with the dilated duct (Figure 2B). A 22-gauge needle was directed through the gastric wall and into the cyst (Figure 2C). Clear, viscous fluid was aspirated and sent for cytologic examination and analysis of tumor markers.

I then proceeded to perform endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (Figure 3). Contrast material was injected through the ampulla into the pancreatic duct, and multiple radiographs of the main duct were obtained. Although contrast material flowed easily into the portions of the dilated duct in the head and body of the pancreas, filling of the duct in the tail could not be achieved. Mucin in the duct may have prevented the retrograde flow of contrast material. There were no filling defects or strictures.

The differential diagnosis in a patient with a diffusely dilated pancreatic duct, a cystic lesion, and a somewhat dilated ampulla includes chronic pancreatitis and intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm. In both conditions, there may be diffuse dilatation of the pancreatic duct throughout the gland, without areas of stricture, as well as cysts or pseudocysts. In chronic pancreatitis, there usually is evidence on ultrasonography of chronic inflammation in the pancreatic parenchyma, consisting either of multiple hypoechoic lesions (representing cystic areas) or of hyperechoic foci (representing scarring and calcifications). This patient had none of those findings, leading me to favor a diagnosis of diffuse intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm. The ampulla may be slightly abnormal in cases of chronic pancreatitis, but a slightly dilated orifice of the ampulla with mucin extruding from it is endoscopically diagnostic of an intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm and is not seen in chronic pancreatitis.

In this patient, we awaited the results of cytopathological examination and analysis of tumor markers in the cyst fluid to confirm the diagnosis and to rule out cancer.

Dr. William R. Brugge's Diagnosis

Intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm of the pancreas.

Pathological Discussion

Dr. Martha B. Pitman: Microscopical examination of the aspirate showed abundant amounts of very thick mucin (Figure 4A), a finding consistent with the gross appearance of the aspirated fluid. Present within this thick mucin were cellular debris, macrophages containing mucin, and a few viable glandular epithelial cells. Some of the epithelial cells were bland, with a distinctive columnar shape and with round, regular basal nuclei and abundant apical cytoplasm, resembling cells of an adenoma (Figure 4B). Other glandular cells, however, were present in small, tightly cohesive, papillary-like clusters with moderate cytologic atypia. The cells had a high nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratio and irregular nuclear membranes — features outside of the range of cytomorphologic features of benign adenoma but insufficient for a diagnosis of adenocarcinoma (Figure 4C). These cytologic features are indicative of a borderline tumor.

The differential diagnosis of a cystic lesion of the pancreas includes pseudocyst and cystic neoplasm. The distinction between these two entities is the most important diagnostic distinction for the pathologist to make in cases of such lesions, since most cystic neoplasms need to be resected, whereas pseudocysts can usually be treated conservatively. Cystic neoplasms include serous cystadenoma, solid pseudopapillary tumor, mucinous cystic tumors (such as mucinous cystic neoplasm and intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm), and solid tumors with cystic degeneration (such as cystic pancreatic endocrine tumor and cystic acinar-cell carcinoma).

A general diagnosis of a neoplastic mucinous cyst is established by the detection of the thick mucin and the identification of mucinous glandular epithelial cells lining the cyst. The thick, viscous nature of the mucin and the amount of it, as well as the presence of degenerated cells and inflammation within the mucin, suggest a mucinous neoplasm as its origin.1 Contamination with material from the gastrointestinal tract is an important consideration in all endoscopic ultrasound-guided biopsies and is a potentially serious pitfall in the evaluation of pancreatic lesions, particularly pseudocysts, by this diagnostic technique. The appearance of the tumor mucin in this case differs from that of gastrointestinal mucin, which tends to be thinner and more wispy and is usually devoid of degenerated epithelial cells and inflammation. The presence of epithelial cells that clearly are not of gastric origin also eliminates pseudocyst from the differential diagnosis, because a pseudocyst would not contain epithelial cells.

The World Health Organization subclassification of mucinous tumors of the pancreas includes two main categories: mucinous cystic neoplasms and intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasms (Table 1).2 The mucinous cystic neoplasms, which are found almost exclusively in women, tend to occur most commonly in the tail of the pancreas and are characterized histologically by the presence of subepithelial, ovarian-type stroma.3 Intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm is a category that has recently clarified older and sometimes misleading terms such as "villous adenoma of the pancreatic duct," "mucin-producing tumor of the pancreatic duct," and "mucinous duct ectasia."2,4,5 The morphologic features of the epithelial cells in the case under discussion suggest the presence of either type of mucinous neoplasm. Although the identification of cytologically atypical glandular cells that are not overtly malignant indicates a tumor of at least borderline malignancy, the absence of obviously malignant cells does not rule out the presence of an intraductal carcinoma, and the presence or absence of invasion cannot be determined by examination of aspirated cyst fluid alone.

Since we know, on the basis of clinical and radiologic information, that this patient has a cystic mass that communicates with a diffusely dilated pancreatic duct, we can combine that knowledge with the cytologic findings to establish a diagnosis of intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm of at least borderline malignancy.

Dr. Kent B. Lewandrowski: A specimen of the cyst fluid was sent for chemical analysis as part of an investigational study at this institution. The first step in the analysis of pancreatic cyst fluid is to measure the amylase level. If it is low or if amylase is undetectable, the possibility of a nonpancreatic cyst should be considered. If the amylase level is elevated or greater than the corresponding level in serum, then a pancreatic lesion is most likely present. High amylase and lipase values in the cyst fluid may suggest the presence of a pancreatic pseudocyst, although a high amylase value is not a reliable finding.

If it is established that the cyst is a pancreatic process, the next test to consider is measurement of the carcinoembryonic antigen level. Low levels indicate the presence of a nonmucinous cyst, such as pseudocyst, serous cystadenoma, or another, less common cystic lesion such as a lymphoepithelial cyst.6 Elevated levels of carcinoembryonic antigen indicate that a mucinous cyst, either benign or malignant, is probably present. A markedly elevated carcinoembryonic antigen level is more consistent with a malignant process than with a benign process, although this finding is not entirely reliable. A number of other possible markers of cancer, including CA 72-4, CA 15-3, and CA 19-9, have been reported to be present in the mucus.6 However, experience with these markers is limited. None of them have been approved by the Food and Drug Administration for the diagnosis of pancreatic cystic lesions, and therefore they should be considered investigational.

In the present case, we received a small volume of thick mucinous material. Owing to its small volume, we limited the analysis to carcinoembryonic antigen and CA 72-4. The carcinoembryonic antigen level was 8960 ng per milliliter and the CA 72-4 level was 400 U per milliliter. Although values in the ductal fluid of patients with intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasms have not been established, the very high level of carcinoembryonic antigen suggests the presence of a mucinous tumor.7,8 The high level of carcinoembryonic antigen, together with an elevated CA 72-4 level, is consistent with a malignant process.

Dr. Nancy Lee Harris (Pathology): Dr. Warshaw, will you discuss the management of intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm and the care of this patient?

Discussion of Management

Dr. Andrew L. Warshaw: This condition was first described in 1982 as "mucinous ductal ectasia."9 This tumor had clearly occurred before 1982 but had not been recognized: when investigators at the Mayo Clinic10 reexamined pancreatic-resection specimens obtained during the 20-year period before 1982, they found the expected number of intraductal papillary mucinous tumors among their archival specimens of cystic tumors and chronic pancreatitis. The types of cystic tumors seen at this hospital from 1979 through 2000 are shown in Figure 5. By 2000, the cumulative prevalence of intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm was 24 percent of the cases in which surgery was performed. It has by now become the most common type of cystic tumor of the pancreas seen at this hospital.

It has been reported that intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm is much more common in men than in women, but in our experience of more than 100 cases, it has occurred somewhat more often in women. Some patients present with pancreatic insufficiency mimicking chronic pancreatitis, whereas others present with pain and episodes of acute pancreatitis, presumably caused by mucus or tumor plugging the ducts. The tumors may be very localized, small "papillomas" or may involve the entire pancreatic ductal system. They tend to spread longitudinally along the duct, long before they spread radially into the parenchyma. Some intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasms produce excessive amounts of mucin, which may choke the duct away from the lesion and may be seen extruding from a dilated ampulla into the duodenum, as Dr. Brugge saw in this patient. It is important to recognize that a dilated portion of a duct does not necessarily contain tumor, since inspissated mucin can dilate a histologically uninvolved portion of duct. Because of its generally indolent progression, intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm has been called the most curable form of pancreatic cancer.

Intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm passes through a long indolent phase, perhaps as long as decades, before cancer develops. This patient's tumor involved the main pancreatic duct, and the presence of a cyst adjacent to the main duct suggests involvement of a branch duct. It has been suggested that intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasms that arise in the branch ducts are more likely to be benign than those involving the main pancreatic duct and perhaps can even be observed rather than resected. In a recent study,11 the prevalence of overt cancer in neoplasms arising in the branch ducts was less than that in neoplasms in the main duct but was still 31 percent. To me, that means that cancer must still be presumed to be present, and in fact, the post-resection survival curves of patients with branch-duct intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm were identical to those of patients with main-duct intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm.

Currently, the only treatment for intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm is surgical resection. A major issue in the treatment of patients with this condition is the extent of resection that is necessary. The current strategy of the surgical group at this hospital is to remove only the part of the pancreas that appears to be involved, according to the imaging studies. We perform a partial pancreatectomy if possible and carefully examine the histologic features of the margins on frozen section. Absence of epithelium at the margin is not sufficient to make a judgment about whether the margin is negative; there must be normal mucosa without mucinous metaplasia.

Recently, prompted by the experience of Hara et al.12 with preoperative peroral pancreatoscopy, we have used intraoperative pancreatoscopy in 17 patients to determine whether we can accurately map the distribution of the tumor. Although we rely on histologic examination of frozen sections to ensure that the resection margin is clear, pancreatoscopy could be useful for verifying that there is no "skip area" of neoplasm left in the pancreatic remnant. Although we have seen cases in which intraluminal villous projections indicate the presence of tumor, we have also found that flat epithelium that looks normal on endoscopy can contain neoplastic changes — even carcinoma in situ. We therefore remain wary of the sensitivity of pancreatoscopy to detect the especially subtle changes of intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm.

We recently evaluated our surgical experience as of a year ago, which then comprised 80 resections for intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm since 1990. Fifty-one of the 80 patients underwent a pancreaticoduodenectomy (a Whipple resection), 12 underwent a total pancreatectomy, and 17 underwent a distal or other partial pancreatectomy. Sixty-five percent of the patients had at least carcinoma in situ, and more than half of those had invasive carcinoma. Of the 80 patients, 63 are currently alive. Sixteen of the 17 patients who died had invasive carcinoma at the time of their resection, and 1 had carcinoma in situ. There have been no deaths from cancer among the patients originally classified as having benign or borderline disease. Our results are similar to those reported by Hara et al.,12 who found cancer-related deaths in patients with invasive cancer but none in patients with carcinoma in situ.

One question has been whether intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm is associated with a field defect that leaves any remaining pancreatic duct at risk for future development of neoplasia. We have not seen recurrences or new intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm in the pancreatic remnants, as long as the margins are clear. However, we advocate continuing surveillance with CT just in case there is subsequent appearance of neoplasm in the pancreatic remnant. At this hospital, resection has been repeated in two patients with positive margins whose cancers recurred in the pancreatic remnant; both now are free of cancer. Thus, repeated resection can cure local recurrences in some cases.

The patient being discussed today had involvement of the entire pancreatic duct. Because of the extent of the tumor, we performed a total pancreaticoduodenectomy. He had an uneventful postoperative course.

Dr. Pitman: A resection specimen consisting of the entire pancreas, distal portion of the stomach, proximal duodenum, and spleen was received in the Department of Pathology for histologic evaluation. The entire pancreatic duct was dilated and filled with mucin, and the mucosal lining was irregular focally. Approximately 4 cm from the pancreatic tail, a 2.5-cm, unilocular, smooth, thin-walled cyst was present adjacent to the dilated duct (Figure 6A). There was no evidence of a mass within the cyst wall or of tumor invasion through the cyst wall.

Histologically, the entire dilated main pancreatic duct was lined by epithelium that ranged from a flat, uniform, mucinous epithelium typical of an adenoma (Figure 6B) to a stratified, tufted, less mucinous, and moderately atypical epithelium typical of a tumor of borderline malignancy (Figure 6C). The cyst was lined by similar epithelium (with features ranging from those of adenoma to those of borderline malignancy) but also contained foci of markedly atypical, highly stratified, tufted epithelium, indicating intraductal carcinoma (Figure 6D). There was also extensive mucinous metaplasia of the intralobular ducts, also known as pancreatic intraepithelial neoplasia, which is the precursor lesion of typical ductal adenocarcinoma13 (Figure 6E).

Dr. Harris: Can you tell us how the patient is doing now?

Dr. Warshaw: He remains well and has no evidence of cancer two years later.

Anatomical Diagnosis

Intraductal papillary mucinous carcinoma of the pancreas, arising in an intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm and involving both the main pancreatic duct and a side branch.
