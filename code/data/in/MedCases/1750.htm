Case 34-2003 — A 45-Year-Old Woman with a Family History of Colonic Polyps and Cancer

Presentation of Case

A 45-year-old woman came to the outpatient clinic for counseling because of concern about a family history of colonic polyps and cancer.

The patient felt well and had had no abdominal pain, change in bowel habits, rectal bleeding, or weight loss. She had had no operations and took no medications. A colonoscopic examination had been performed for screening purposes four years earlier, and no polyps had been identified. She did not smoke and rarely consumed alcohol.

The patient's mother was 80 years old and had been given a diagnosis of colon cancer at 75 years of age. The patient's father was 74 years old. At 53 years of age, he had had recurrent episodes of pancreatitis, and a workup had revealed a large villous adenoma of the ampulla of Vater, which was surgically resected. A recurrence of the lesion necessitated a Whipple procedure that was performed at 56 years of age. Colonoscopy was performed, and more than 50 small adenomas were identified in the right side of the colon. Two large adenomas measuring more than 1 cm in diameter were seen, one at the hepatic flexure and one in the rectosigmoid colon. When the patient's father was 57 years old, a subtotal colectomy with an ileosigmoid anastomosis was performed. Multiple fundic-gland polyps were also identified in his stomach. He had been followed thereafter with sigmoidoscopic examinations every one to two years, and several adenomas had been removed each time.

The patient had three paternal aunts; breast cancer had developed in one at 80 years of age, bladder cancer had been diagnosed in another at 79 years of age, and the third was alive and healthy at 79 years of age. The patient's paternal grandfather had died at 72 years of age without a diagnosis of cancer, but her paternal grandfather's sister had died of colon cancer in her 30s. Her paternal grandmother had died at 35 years of age from unknown causes.

The patient's brother had had a normal colonoscopic examination at 35 years of age. However, multiple adenomas were later identified in the cecum when he was 45 years old, and a right hemicolectomy was performed. The pathological analysis confirmed the presence of 50 to 100 diminutive adenomas in the cecum and the right side of the colon. Annual follow-up colonoscopic examinations revealed several diminutive adenomas; an endoscopic examination of the stomach and duodenum revealed fundic-gland polyps in the stomach and a tubular adenoma in the duodenum. The patient's sister was 46 years old, had no known medical problems, and had never undergone colonoscopy.

The patient had two children, 13 and 11 years of age, both of whom were healthy. The physical examination and results of routine laboratory studies performed by the patient's primary care physician were normal.

Syndromes of colonic polyposis are classified into two types: nonadenomatous and adenomatous. Nonadenomatous polyposis syndromes include Peutz–Jegher's syndrome, Cowden's syndrome, juvenile polyposis syndrome, and Cronkhite–Canada syndrome, and they are distinguished from adenomatous polyposis syndromes by the morphologic features of the polyps (i.e., hamartomatous polyps or juvenile polyps) and the presence and type of extracolonic manifestations.1

Familial adenomatous polyposis is the best-recognized adenomatous polyposis syndrome. It can be further classified into two types: classic familial adenomatous polyposis and attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis. Classic familial adenomatous polyposis is characterized by the presence of numerous polypoid adenomas — usually numbering in the thousands — located throughout the colon.1,2 Attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis is characterized by the presence of fewer colonic polyps (usually fewer than 100), which are often flat and tend to be located proximal to the splenic flexure.1,2,3,4,5

Extracolonic tumors are found in both disorders. Gastric fundic-gland polyps, duodenal adenomas, and ampullary adenomas are the most common, and the onset of fundic-gland polyps may actually precede that of colonic adenomas in patients with attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis.4 In the classic form of the disorder, papillary thyroid tumors, abdominal desmoid tumors, osteomas, epidermoid cysts, and hepatoblastomas also occur, but these tumors are uncommon in patients with the attenuated form of the disorder. The eponym Gardner's syndrome was originally applied to cases in which there were bone and soft-tissue abnormalities in addition to the phenotype of familial adenomatous polyposis.1,2 Because the genetic basis of familial adenomatous polyposis is identical to that of Gardner's syndrome, this distinction does not appear to be meaningful.

The pathological features of the polyps and their anatomical distribution in this family are most consistent with attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis.

Differential Diagnosis

Dr. Daniel C. Chung: The patient in this case is known to me, and I am aware of the diagnosis. The most compelling feature is the family history of multiple adenomatous polyps (Figure 3). The overall prevalence of colonic adenomas among persons 50 years of age is estimated to be less than 25 percent.6 Three or fewer synchronous adenomas are typically identified. The finding of more than 50 polyps is consistent with a polyposis syndrome, and its presence in both the patient's father (family member II-2) and her brother (III-1) indicates an inheritance pattern that is autosomal dominant. The colon cancer diagnosed in the patient's mother (II-1) at 75 years of age probably represents a sporadic case, since she did not have multiple polyps and there is no compelling history of colon cancer among her relatives.

Approximately 25 percent of cases of colon cancer are associated with a familial predisposition. A recent meta-analysis estimated that the relative risk of colon cancer is 2.25 if colon cancer is diagnosed in a first-degree relative7 and 4.25 if there are two affected first-degree relatives. If these diagnoses are made at an early age (before 45 years), the relative risk is estimated to be 3.87. Similarly, the relative risk is 1.99 if a first-degree relative has been given a diagnosis of a colonic adenoma. The highest risk of colon cancer, however, is that found in families that exhibit features of familial adenomatous polyposis or hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer, both of which are autosomal dominant disorders.8 Each family member has a 50 percent chance of inheriting the disorder, and among those who do inherit it, the risk of colon cancer approaches 100 percent.

Familial Adenomatous Polyposis and Attenuated Familial Adenomatous Polyposis

The diagnosis of familial adenomatous polyposis is straightforward; the characteristic carpeting of the colon with hundreds or thousands of polyps may be observed by the second or third decade of life. The delayed appearance of only a moderate number of polyps is seen in the attenuated form of the disorder. This form was initially described as the hereditary flat-adenoma syndrome, but it has since been recognized that the adenomas may be either flat or polypoid.4,9,10 The natural history of attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis is continuing to be defined, but the risk of colon cancer among persons with the disorder is estimated to be between 80 and 100 percent.

The diagnosis of attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis may be overlooked for several reasons. The polyposis is not florid, and the age at onset is late. In addition, there is marked phenotypic heterogeneity, not only among different kindreds, but also within an individual kindred. Some persons may present with as few as 1 to 10 polyps or with colon cancer as late as 60 to 70 years of age. Thus, the clinical features may be indistinguishable from those of sporadic cases. For these reasons, attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis is probably underdiagnosed, and its true prevalence is unknown. A heightened clinical awareness, in combination with a careful family history taking, is the essential first step in the recognition of this syndrome.

Genetic Basis of Familial Adenomatous Polyposis

Familial adenomatous polyposis and attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis both result from germ-line mutations in the APC tumor-suppressor gene, which was one of the first inherited cancer-susceptibility genes to be cloned, in 1991. Mutations in APC activate the Wnt signaling pathway, which controls cell-cycle progression and apoptosis. Nearly 400 different germ-line mutations have been identified throughout the APC gene,11 and more than 80 percent result in a premature stop codon, which leads to the synthesis of a truncated protein that is nonfunctional.

There are fascinating correlations between the location of the APC mutation and the clinical phenotype (Figure 4). Thirty-four different mutations causing attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis have been reported to date, and they are clustered either at the far 5' end or the 3' end of the coding region of the APC gene.10 In contrast, the mutations involved in classic familial adenomatous polyposis are located in the central region, and mutations between codons 1250 and 1464 are associated with particularly severe polyposis. Abdominal desmoid tumors are more likely to occur in persons with mutations between codons 1445 and 1578. A point mutation in which lysine is substituted for isoleucine at codon 1307 (I1307K) has been identified in 6 percent of the Ashkenazi Jewish population and is associated with an increase by a factor of less than two in the risk of cancer without accompanying polyposis.12

The molecular mechanisms that explain why certain APC mutations result in a classic phenotype and others in an attenuated phenotype are currently being elucidated. Most models are predicated on the "two-hit hypothesis" — which states that both alleles of APC must be inactivated in order to initiate tumorigenesis. In classic familial adenomatous polyposis, affected persons inherit one mutant APC gene that produces a nonfunctional protein, and the second normal, or wild-type, allele is typically inactivated by a chromosomal deletion, leading to a loss of heterozygosity. In some cases, the mutant APC protein may even behave in a dominant negative manner, functionally inactivating the remaining wild-type protein.13 In contrast, mutations that give rise to attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis result in proteins that appear to be partly functional.14 Consequently, somatic mutations must occur to inactivate not only the wild-type allele but also the allele with the mutation for attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis (Figure 5).15 The additional time required to acquire these two somatic mutations is predicted to be far greater than that required to inactivate one allele, and this difference may account for the later presentation in persons with attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis, as compared with that in classic familial adenomatous polyposis. The molecular basis for the right-sided predominance of polyps, however, is entirely unknown.

Hereditary Nonpolyposis Colorectal Cancer

An alternative diagnostic consideration in this case is the hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer syndrome, another autosomal dominant condition that is characterized by the occurrence of a limited number of adenomas (Table 1). The clinical and genetic features of hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer have been detailed elsewhere.8,16 As in attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis, the adenomas and cancers are located preferentially in the right side of the colon. However, the mean age at the diagnosis of colon cancer is lower (45 years), the number of adenomas is much smaller, and the polyps do not occur in a carpet-like pattern. According to the Amsterdam criteria of 1991, a diagnosis of hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer can be made when three or more family members have a diagnosis of colorectal cancer, when one of those family members is a first-degree relative of the other two, and when one of the cases is diagnosed before 50 years of age. There is a strong association between hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer and tumors of the endometrium, urinary tract, ovary, stomach, and biliary tract, but not between this syndrome and fundic-gland polyps or ampullary tumors. For all these reasons, this diagnosis is unlikely to be correct in this case.

Colonic Adenomas and Germ-Line Mutations in MYH

A recently described syndrome of multiple colonic adenomas due to alterations in the MYH gene also deserves consideration.17 MYH repairs oxidized deoxyguanine nucleotides, and in the study by Sieber et al.,17 29 percent of persons who had more than 15 colonic adenomas were found to harbor homozygous germ-line mutations in MYH. Some of these persons even had features of classic familial adenomatous polyposis. Much remains to be learned about the full clinical spectrum of this disorder, but its autosomal recessive pattern of inheritance makes it an unlikely diagnosis in this case.

Summary and Diagnosis

In this patient's family, the autosomal dominant pattern of inheritance, the right-sided oligopolyposis occurring during the fifth or sixth decade of life, and the associated ampullary tumors and fundic-gland polyps all point convincingly to the diagnosis of attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis. There is a clear-cut pattern of inheritance in two generations (Figure 3). The penetrance of classic familial adenomatous polyposis is 100 percent. The patient's father (family member II-2) may have had a new mutation, since neither of his parents had polyposis or colon cancer. Such new cases account for approximately 20 percent of families with familial adenomatous polyposis. However, the possibility that the patient's paternal grandfather was a silent carrier cannot be entirely ruled out, since the penetrance of attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis has not yet been defined. This patient has a 50 percent risk of having attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis. Although she had a normal colonoscopy four years before presentation, this does not rule out the diagnosis, because the age of onset of polyps among persons with this disorder varies widely. A more definitive assessment of the patient's risk has obvious implications not only for her, but also for her children.

Dr. Daniel C. Chung's Diagnosis

Attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis with a germ-line mutation in the APC gene.

Discussion of Management

Dr. Chung: The care of patients with known or suspected mutations in the APC gene involves intensive surveillance for cancer, chemoprevention, and more recently, genetic testing. Annual screening for colonic polyps and cancer is required for all known or suspected carriers of attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis. Colonoscopy rather than sigmoidoscopy is necessary because of the right-sided predominance of the polyps. Because of the variable age at the onset of polyps, there is no standardized age at which to begin colonoscopic screening. One set of conservative guidelines recommends beginning as early as 15 to 25 years of age,10,18 and screening must continue throughout life. Because of the heterogeneity in the number of polyps in persons with attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis, prophylactic colectomy is not considered to be mandatory, as it is for persons with classic familial adenomatous polyposis. However, in cases in which all polyps cannot be removed endoscopically or in which cancer is diagnosed, a subtotal colectomy with ileorectal anastomosis is recommended, followed by continued endoscopic surveillance of any remaining rectal mucosa.

The second leading cause of cancer-related death in patients with familial adenomatous polyposis is duodenal and ampullary cancer. Thus, lifelong surveillance for cancers that can arise in these extracolonic sites is also required. It is recommended that an upper endoscopy be performed every one to three years, with a biopsy of the ampulla, even if it appears to be normal. Fundic-gland polyps have traditionally been thought to have no malignant potential, but this premise is being challenged, since reports have suggested that there may be an increased risk of gastric adenocarcinoma among patients with attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis.19 These guidelines apply not only to known mutation carriers but also to family members who are at risk but whose carrier status remains unknown.

Chemopreventive Agents

Adenomatous polyps and colorectal cancers greatly overexpress cyclooxygenase-2,20 which regulates prostaglandin synthesis. Overproduction of prostaglandin E2, in particular, may be key in the pathogenesis of colon cancer.21 Agents that inhibit the enzymatic activity of cyclooxygenase-2 (sulindac and celecoxib) have been tested as chemopreventive agents in randomized, controlled trials involving patients with familial adenomatous polyposis who had a residual rectum. Both of these agents have been shown to reduce the number and size of polyps,22,23,24 although they do not appear to prevent the initial formation of colonic polyps25 or to have as potent an effect on duodenal polyps.26 It must be emphasized that these agents cannot be considered to be definitive therapy and will not replace endoscopic surveillance and carefully timed surgery. Nevertheless, they have assumed an important adjunctive role in the management of familial adenomatous polyposis and attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis.

Genetic Testing in Classic or Attenuated Familial Adenomatous Polyposis

The most commonly used genetic test detects germ-line alterations in the APC gene in DNA from peripheral-blood mononuclear cells by directly sequencing all 15 APC exons. An alternative is the protein-truncation test.27 In this test, the gene is transcribed into RNA in vitro and translated into protein, and then the protein products are electrophoretically separated according to size. If there is a premature stop codon, a truncated protein product is generated. The protein-truncation test can miss mutations that may be deleterious but do not result in the production of a truncated protein.

The diagnosis of attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis can be made on the basis of either clinical or genetic criteria. When the family history, clinical features, and pathological findings are classic, as in this case, the diagnosis is straightforward. However, for equivocal cases, genetic testing is a powerful tool that can provide a definitive diagnosis. In this case, the role of genetic testing is not to establish the diagnosis, but to assess the risk of colon cancer in family members whose carrier status is unknown. Without genetic testing, this patient is committed to a lifetime of endoscopic surveillance. Consequently, genetic testing is now considered to be the standard of care in the management of familial adenomatous polyposis and attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis.

Genetic Testing and Results

Ms. Kristen M. Shannon: For all inherited cancer syndromes, the optimal approach is to test an affected family member (the proband) first. If a mutation is identified, then an asymptomatic relative can undergo testing, and the results of such testing will be nearly 100 percent accurate for defining risk. In less than 20 percent of cases with clinical features of familial adenomatous polyposis, no mutation is identified in the proband; this result is uninformative. Potential explanations for an uninformative test result include mutations that are located in regions that are not sequenced (such as introns or the promoter) or involvement of yet-unidentified genes. If an uninformative test result is obtained, no further testing should be performed in any family member. Although the failure to identify an APC mutation in the proband does not mean that the diagnosis of familial adenomatous polyposis or attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis has been ruled out, it does mean that genetic testing for the kindred is not an option, and all family members will require intensive surveillance for cancer.

In families in which no affected relative is available for testing, consideration can be given to the direct testing of a family member who is at risk for carrying a mutation. The limitation of this approach is that the failure to identify a mutation cannot be interpreted as a true negative in the absence of a known mutation in the family. Given the nuances of genetic testing, it is imperative that the ordering practitioner be fully versed in the indications for testing and in the interpretation of test results. A retrospective analysis indicated that only 68 percent of the physicians who ordered an APC gene test interpreted the results correctly.28

The benefits of genetic testing include the elimination of uncertainty and the provision of an accurate assessment of the risk of cancer among both carriers and noncarriers of mutations. This approach is cost effective, particularly when it is initiated at an early age.29 These benefits must be weighed against the potential risks. Emotional distress can occur in those found to carry a mutation as well as in those who are found not to carry a mutation — so-called survivor guilt. Another perceived risk is that of genetic discrimination. In this country, however, there do not appear to be any well-documented cases of discrimination based on the results of genetic testing for inherited cancer syndromes.30,31 In addition, 44 states have enacted legislation to protect people against discrimination by employers or health insurers on the basis of the results of genetic testing.

Genetic counseling is an essential component of the genetic-testing process. A counseling session includes discussions of the patterns of inheritance, the logistics of the genetic test, the possible test results, the pros and cons of testing, informed consent, and emotional support. We explore the effects of the diagnosis on both the patient and the entire family. More than 90 percent of the patients who have undergone such counseling and testing for other inherited cancer syndromes have been satisfied with their decision to do so.32,33 A genetic-counseling session could also help patients with familial adenomatous polyposis to assess the desirability of the use of two controversial applications: prenatal testing and preimplantation diagnosis.34

The patient's father received genetic counseling and underwent APC gene testing with the protein-truncation test. He had a germ-line mutation that resulted in a premature truncation at the 5' end of the gene. Subsequent sequencing pinpointed the truncating mutation to codon 169, confirming the diagnosis of attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis. The patient herself subsequently underwent testing. Because a mutation had been identified in her father, she did not require analysis of the entire APC gene, and the mutational analysis was limited to codon 169. She did not carry the truncating mutation, which indicates that she does not have attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis.

Neither the patient nor her children will require aggressive surveillance for cancer. Genetic testing can be recommended to the patient's older sister (family member III-2), as well as to her brother's children (IV-1, IV-2, and IV-3). It is not necessary to test the patient's brother, since he is by definition a carrier of the gene.

Dr. Kurt J. Isselbacher (Internal Medicine): Do you think we will soon be able to establish a molecular diagnosis for the varieties of colon cancer, rather than a pathologically or clinically descriptive one?

Dr. Chung: Colon cancer is much more heterogeneous on a genetic level than it is histologically. Three major genetic pathways that lead to colon cancer are currently recognized: chromosomal instability, microsatellite instability, and hypermethylation. Within each of these pathways, there are further subdivisions with respect to the specific genes that are altered. It will become possible to identify such a molecular signature for individual tumors, which will undoubtedly have important prognostic and therapeutic implications.

Dr. Nancy Lee Harris (Pathology): If the two-hit mechanism of carcinogenesis requires a second APC mutation in every polyp, this phenomenon must occur thousands of times in every patient with familial adenomatous polyposis. Does the APC gene undergo mutations that frequently in normal persons with no apparent effect, or does the germ-line mutation somehow increase the likelihood of a mutation in the other allele?

Dr. Chung: Loss of heterozygosity of the APC gene occurs in many polyps in patients with familial adenomatous polyposis, but not in all such polyps. Interestingly, certain APC germ-line mutations are associated with a loss of heterozygosity, whereas other APC germ-line mutations are associated with small insertions, deletions, or point mutations in the second APC allele. This variability is consistent with the evolving notion that the nature of the first hit directly influences the type of the second hit. The mechanism for this phenomenon remains to be defined, but in familial adenomatous polyposis, it may revolve around a newly recognized role for the APC gene in the regulation of chromosomal stability.

Final Diagnosis

Attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis syndrome with a germ-line truncating mutation of the APC gene at codon 169. The patient does not have the mutation.
