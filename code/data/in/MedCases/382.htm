Case 23-2003 — A 79-Year-Old Woman with Gastric Lymphoma and Erosive Mucosal and Cutaneous Lesions
A. Razzaque Ahmed, M.D., Mathew M. Avram, M.D., J.D., and Lyn M. Duncan, M.D.

Presentation of Case

A 79-year-old woman with a gastric B-cell lymphoma of the mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) type was admitted to the hospital because of erosive mucosal and cutaneous lesions.

The patient had been well until 14 months before admission, when she began to have cramping abdominal pain and to pass black, tarry stools. Endoscopic ultrasonographic examination of the upper gastrointestinal tract to the second part of the duodenum, performed 13 months before admission, revealed a large, submucosal, noncircumferential mass, 6 by 3 cm, involving the cardia and gastroesophageal junction, without associated bleeding or ulceration; the mass was well defined and did not appear to be locally invasive. Examination of a specimen obtained by fine-needle aspiration showed morphologic and immunophenotypic evidence of an extranodal, marginal-zone, B-cell lymphoma of the MALT type.

A complete blood count and the results of routine blood-chemistry tests were normal. A test for antibodies against Helicobacter pylori was negative. Thoracic and abdominopelvic computed tomographic (CT) scanning revealed an enhancing, solid mass that was 6.8 by 4.0 cm and had a lobulated contour. The mass protruded into the lumen of the stomach but did not obstruct it. There were multiple enlarged lymph nodes in the gastrohepatic ligament and porta hepatis; the largest was 2.1 cm in diameter. There were three subpleural nodules, each 2 to 3 mm in diameter, in the right lung, but there was no hilar or mediastinal lymphadenopathy. Whole-body scanning with gallium-67 and positron-emission tomographic scanning revealed no evidence of disease outside the abdomen.

Beginning 10 months before admission, the patient received radiation therapy to the upper abdomen in 20 fractions, each 180 cGy, over a period of 6 weeks; she tolerated therapy with the help of prochlorperazine and ondansetron. She then decided against further treatment, and she also became heavily involved in the home care of her husband, who had oxygen-dependent chronic obstructive pulmonary disease.

Two months before admission, painful oral ulcerations that involved much of the oropharynx developed. Fluconazole was administered, without improvement. One month before admission, the patient was admitted to another hospital because of asthma. Methylprednisolone and levofloxacin were given for six days, and the bronchospasm improved. Three weeks before admission, a rash appeared over the patient's entire body; it consisted of round, erythematous lesions, each 5 to 10 mm in diameter, that enlarged and coalesced into ulcerations with scabs that adhered to her clothing. She again was admitted to the other hospital, where acyclovir given intravenously for 10 days had no effect. Her eyes became inflamed, and a culture specimen yielded a growth of methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus; topical erythromycin was administered. She was admitted to this hospital.

The patient had retired from an office position. She had a long history of cigarette smoking and consumed one alcoholic drink daily. She had not recently lost weight or had nausea, vomiting, or peripheral edema. She had undergone an appendectomy at the age of 14 years, a cholecystectomy at the age of 49, and a simple right-sided mastectomy for ductal carcinoma in situ 2 years before admission. She had a history of asthma that had begun at the age of 40 and had had atrial fibrillation with congestive heart failure six years before admission. Her medications comprised nebulized salmeterol and fluticasone, amiodarone, tamoxifen, omeprazole, and vitamins. A brother had died of a brain tumor at the age of 27 years, and an elderly aunt had been treated for lymphoma and pancreatic cancer.

The temperature was 37.9°C, the pulse was 108 beats per minute, and the respiratory rate was 24 breaths per minute. The blood pressure was 110/50 mm Hg.

Over the patient's trunk, arms, and legs were multiple round, erythematous plaques, some of which were tender; they had coalesced into larger plaques that did not blanch completely on pressure (Figure 1). The lesions had ulcerations in the center, with mucoid secretions that adhered to the patient's clothing; some of the lesions contained scabs, and others bled slightly. Her eyelids were edematous, and her conjunctivae were injected, with greenish secretions. Buccal erosions were also present. The site of the right-sided mastectomy was unremarkable, and the left breast was normal. The lungs and heart were normal. The liver edge descended 2 to 3 cm; the spleen was not felt, and no mass or ascites was detected. There was edema (+) of both legs, with intact pulses. Rectal examination revealed no abnormalities; a stool specimen was negative for occult blood.

Differential Diagnosis

Dr. A. Razzaque Ahmed: May we see the radiographic studies and the clinical photographs of the skin lesions?

Dr. Jason L. Kelly (Radiology): A contrast-enhanced image from the CT scan of the abdomen obtained 13 months before the current admission revealed a large, lobulated mass in the lesser curvature of the stomach and several enlarged lymph nodes in the perigastric region and the gastrohepatic ligament. The CT scan obtained on the current admission showed a reduction in the size of the mass and the sizes of the lymph nodes.

Dr. Mathew M. Avram: A photograph of the patient's back (Figure 1A) shows numerous erosions, 0.5 to 3.0 cm in diameter, with pink, moist bases, numerous sites of pinpoint bleeding, and peripheral collarettes of scale, suggesting that the erosions had developed from bullae that had ruptured. In addition, there are multiple poorly defined pink macules. On the chest there was a large, confluent erosion similar to those on the back. There are numerous erosions on the lips and tongue (Figure 1B), as well as erosive gingivitis. There are erosions and erythema of the conjunctivae, with purulent secretions (Figure 1C).

Dr. Ahmed: The clinical manifestations of this case require us to focus on four categories of diseases: infections, cutaneous involvement by lymphoma, mucocutaneous diseases not associated with malignant tumors, and mucocutaneous diseases associated with malignant tumors. The mucocutaneous disease in this case probably does not have an infectious cause, because there was no response to antifungal or antiviral therapies and because there was no systemic involvement. It is also unlikely to be a sign of cutaneous involvement by lymphoma. Cutaneous lymphomas have morphologic features unlike those in this case and are not associated with such extensive and rapidly developing mucosal disease. They form multiple, large, infiltrated plaques, papules or nodules, which may be either normal or violaceous in color or purpuric.

Of the third category, mucocutaneous diseases not related to a neoplasm, the most relevant disorders are erythema multiforme, the Stevens–Johnson syndrome, and toxic epidermal necrolysis.1,2 Erythema multiforme is an acute or recurrent, usually self-limited disorder that results in cutaneous plaques and target-shaped lesions that are usually acral in distribution. Oral lesions may occur. The disease is most often due to herpes simplex virus infection. In the current case, the widespread involvement of the skin, the progressive nature of the process, and the absence of a response to antiviral therapy make erythema multiforme an unlikely diagnosis. The Stevens–Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis are drug-related and cause extensive, necrotizing cutaneous and mucosal lesions. In this case, the polymorphic appearance of the skin involvement, the lack of distinctive necrosis and sloughing, and the absence of a history of drug administration decrease but do not eliminate the likelihood of toxic epidermal necrolysis.

The fourth category, mucocutaneous paraneoplastic syndromes associated with hematologic cancers, includes neutrophilic dermatosis (e.g., Sweet's syndrome and pyoderma gangrenosum), reactive erythemas (e.g., erythroderma and exfoliative dermatitis), vascular dermatosis (e.g., vasculitis and erythromelalgia), papulosquamous disorders (e.g., acquired ichthyosis), and vesiculobullous diseases.3 Except for vesiculobullous diseases, these possibilities are unlikely diagnoses in this case, given the patient's history, her clinical presentation, and the appearance of her skin lesions. The focus of the differential diagnosis in this case is therefore on vesiculobullous diseases. Three entities need to be considered: pemphigus, pemphigoid, and paraneoplastic pemphigus (Table 3).

Pemphigus

Pemphigus is a group of blistering diseases characterized by acantholysis (loss of cell adhesion) and by in vivo deposition of autoantibodies against desmoglein.4 In pemphigus foliaceus, the acantholysis occurs in the upper epidermis and the autoantibody is targeted to desmoglein 1. Pemphigus foliaceus involves only the skin. In pemphigus vulgaris, the acantholysis occurs in the deeper layers of the epidermis, and autoantibodies target desmogleins 1 and 3. Pemphigus vulgaris affects both the skin and the mucosa, although some patients have only mucosal disease. In both of these diseases, examination of perilesional skin by direct immunofluorescence shows, in almost all patients, deposition of IgG on the surfaces of keratinocytes, and indirect immunofluorescence detects the autoantibody in the serum.

Both pemphigus foliaceus and pemphigus vulgaris have been reported in association with cancer,5 but the association is more common with pemphigus vulgaris. The most common malignant tumors in patients with pemphigus are Kaposi's sarcoma and lymphoid neoplasms, including Hodgkin's and non-Hodgkin's lymphomas and chronic lymphocytic leukemia.

In the case under discussion, the presence of marked mucosal disease rules out the diagnosis of pemphigus foliaceus. The polymorphic nature and rapid and progressive onset of the cutaneous involvement would be unusual in pemphigus vulgaris, but direct and indirect immunofluorescence analysis would be necessary to rule it out.

Pemphigoid

Pemphigoid4 is a group of diseases that typically affect the elderly and that are characterized by the presence of autoantibodies to a component of the basement-membrane zone. Histologic examination reveals subepidermal blisters and a mixed inflammatory-cell infiltrate in the dermis. Bullous pemphigoid is characterized by large, tense bullae in an acral distribution; mucosal involvement is rare. Mucous-membrane pemphigoid, also referred to as cicatricial pemphigoid, invariably involves one or multiple mucous membranes, involves the skin only occasionally, and is distinguished from other blistering diseases by the fact that as the blisters heal there is considerable scarring.6

In bullous pemphigoid and mucous-membrane pemphigoid, IgG and C3 are deposited in the basement-membrane zone and can be visualized by direct immunofluorescence. Patients with bullous pemphigoid have circulating antibodies to a 230-kD desmoplakin protein (bullous pemphigoid antigen 1) and a 180-kD hemidesmosomal protein (bullous pemphigoid antigen 2). Serum from patients with cicatricial pemphigoid has antibodies to bullous pemphigoid antigen 2, human 4 integrin, and possibly other, unidentified basement-membrane zone and adhesion molecules.

In the case under discussion, the presence of mucosal disease makes bullous pemphigoid an unlikely diagnosis. The extensive and persistent polymorphic cutaneous involvement makes mucous-membrane pemphigoid unlikely, but it would be prudent to rule it out by histologic and immunopathological examination of the skin and serum.

Paraneoplastic Pemphigus

Anhalt et al.7 first described paraneoplastic pemphigus as a distinct entity in 1990 and provided criteria for the diagnosis. Camisa and Helm8 later revised these criteria in order to simplify and facilitate the diagnosis (Table 4). These guidelines propose that all three major criteria and at least two minor criteria should be met before the disease is diagnosed. The most characteristic clinical feature of paraneoplastic pemphigus is intractable, painful stomatitis affecting the entire oral cavity and the lips.7,9 It is the most consistent feature of this disease and usually is the first symptom, and it is often resistant to therapy. Conjunctival involvement is present in approximately two thirds of patients and may be severe, causing pseudomembranous conjunctivitis, symblepharon, and marked visual impairment. Anogenital involvement, particularly of the penis, is common. Esophageal erosions can be present.

Skin involvement is reported in almost all patients, with polymorphic lesions affecting more than half the body surface. The lesions may include flaccid or tense bullae, with or without erosions; lichenoid lesions like those seen in erythema multiforme; and confluent erythema. The cutaneous lesions in an individual patient may have different morphologic features at a single point in time or at various intervals over the course of the disease. The trunk and the proximal portions of the arms and legs are the most frequently involved areas. Palmar, plantar, or paronychial involvement is often observed.

Although respiratory infections can complicate paraneoplastic pemphigus, direct involvement of the tracheobronchial tree can also occur, causing obstructive respiratory failure and death.10 Bronchoscopic examination may reveal inflammation, vesicles, or erosions.11 Paraneoplastic pemphigus may be associated with brochiolitis obliterans with organizing pneumonia, particularly in young patients with Castleman's disease.12

The histologic features of paraneoplastic pemphigus include both intraepidermal acantholysis and suprabasilar clefting.13 Direct-immunofluorescence studies of skin-biopsy specimens typically show deposition of IgG, with or without complement, both on the surfaces of keratinocytes and on the basement-membrane zone.14 Indirect immunofluorescence studies performed with the use of stratified squamous epithelium (such as monkey esophagus or bovine tongue) as a substrate can detect antibodies in serum from patients with pemphigus vulgaris, pemphigus foliaceus, or paraneoplastic pemphigus. In contrast, urinary-bladder epithelium contains desmoplakins but not desmogleins and is therefore a suitable substrate for distinguishing paraneoplastic pemphigus from these other diseases. Immunoprecipitation or immunofluorescence studies can detect autoantibodies to plectin, desmoplakins I and II, envoplakin, periplakin, bullous pemphigoid antigen 2, desmogleins 1 and 3, and an unknown, 170-kD protein in serum from patients with paraneoplastic pemphigus.14,15

Seventy to 75 percent of patients with paraneoplastic pemphigus are known to have cancer at the time of the diagnosis, whereas in other patients cancer is discovered subsequently.7,9 By far the most common associations are with B-cell lymphomas, leukemias, and Castleman's disease (Table 5). Castleman's disease is essentially the only disorder associated with paraneoplastic pemphigus in patients younger than 20 years of age.9 Common tumors such as adenocarcinoma of the breast, bowel, or lung or basal-cell or squamous-cell carcinoma of the skin have not been frequently associated with paraneoplastic pemphigus.9

In patients with a resectable lesion such as Castleman's disease or thymoma, complete resection of the tumor is generally accompanied by considerable improvement and, in some cases, remission.9 Unfortunately, in patients with malignant neoplasms, treatment of the cancer is often not associated with improvement of the paraneoplastic pemphigus, which is often very resistant to therapy; mortality has approached 90 percent in many studies. Death has been attributed to multiple factors, including respiratory failure, sepsis, gastrointestinal bleeding, and multiorgan failure.9

My clinical diagnosis in this case is paraneoplastic pemphigus, but pemphigus vulgaris, cicatricial pemphigoid, and toxic epidermal necrolysis must be ruled out before the diagnosis can be established with certainty. A biopsy of the skin with appropriate histopathological and immunopathological examination would confirm the diagnosis.

Dr. Robert T. McCluskey (Pathology): Dr. Ryan, would you describe your thoughts about this patient before the diagnostic procedure?

Dr. David P. Ryan (Medical Oncology): The patient's gastric MALT lymphoma did not respond to therapy with antibiotics and responded only partly to radiation therapy, with persistent MALT lymphoma observed on subsequent examination of biopsy specimens of the stomach. Nonetheless, she felt well and opted for observation rather than surgical resection of the mass. She did well until the oral and cutaneous lesions developed, at which time her disease was restaged and CT scanning showed persistence of the gastric mass. We were concerned about paraneoplastic pemphigus and also about the possibility of lymphomatous involvement of the skin and mucous membranes. We consulted Dr. Avram, of the Department of Dermatology, and he performed a skin biopsy.

Dr. McCluskey: Dr. Avram, would you discuss your thoughts about this case before the biopsy results were obtained?

Dr. Avram: Our differential diagnosis was very similar to that proposed by Dr. Ahmed. We believed that the patient had either pemphigus vulgaris or paraneoplastic pemphigus. Although paraneoplastic pemphigus is the less common of the two disorders, the severity and persistence of the mucosal and cutaneous involvement, together with the presence of persistent B-cell lymphoma, led us to favor that diagnosis.

Clinical Diagnosis

Paraneoplastic pemphigus.

Pathological Discussion

Dr. Lyn M. Duncan: The diagnosis of gastric marginal-zone B-cell lymphoma of the MALT type was established by a combination of morphologic and flow-cytometric analysis of endoscopically obtained fine-needle aspirates. The specimens predominantly contained small lymphoid cells with slightly irregular nuclei and abundant cytoplasm (known as marginal-zone B cells) and plasma cells; a few large lymphoid cells were also present. Flow-cytometric analysis revealed a population of lambda light-chain–restricted B cells expressing CD20 and CD19 but not CD5, CD10, CD43, or CD23. These results, together with the clinical and morphologic findings, were diagnostic of extranodal marginal-zone B-cell lymphoma of the MALT type, also known as MALT lymphoma.

The diagnostic procedures were punch biopsies of the skin. Biopsy specimens of skin from the left arm and skin from the left thigh were sent for routine histologic processing and direct immunofluorescence staining, respectively. The skin-biopsy specimen from the left arm revealed a blistering process characterized by detachment of the epidermis from the dermis (Figure 2A). The dermis was denuded of epithelium except for a focus along one margin, where a few keratinocytes remained attached to the surface. The detached epidermal blister roof had an intact epidermal surface with acantholysis of keratinocytes in the lower layers of the stratum spinosum (Figure 2B). These findings are typical of paraneoplastic pemphigus, in which there is both detachment of cells from one another (acantholysis and suprabasal clefting) and focal detachment from the basement membrane (subepidermal clefting). These findings contrast with those in cases of pemphigus, in which there is only suprabasal acantholysis and clefting, and pemphigoid, in which the clefting is only subepidermal (Table 3 and Figure 3). The loss of adherence both between the basal-layer keratinocytes and the dermis and between the individual keratinocytes of the lower epidermis is consistent with a process that affects proteins that are integral to desmosomes, which mediate the cohesion of the cells to one another, and to hemidesmosomes, which in turn mediate the attachment of keratinocytes to the basement-membrane zone. Other features that distinguish paraneoplastic pemphigus from pemphigus vulgaris include the presence of apoptosis of keratinocytes and often a more prominent lichenoid inflammatory infiltrate in the dermis.

  		Figure 3. Pathophysiology of Autoimmune Blistering Diseases of the Skin and Mucosae.

Normal epidermis (Panel A) contains desmosomes that mediate attachment between keratinocytes and hemidesmosomes that mediate attachment between keratinocytes and the basement membrane. In pemphigus (Panel B), antibodies to desmosomal proteins are associated with the detachment of keratinocytes from one another (i.e., acantholysis), producing a suprabasal cleft and a netlike pattern of keratinocyte cell-surface staining on direct immunofluorescence. In pemphigoid (Panel C), antibodies to hemidesmosomal proteins are associated with the detachment of keratinocytes from the basement membrane, resulting in a subepidermal cleft and a linear basement-membrane–zone pattern of staining on direct immunofluorescence. In paraneoplastic pemphigus (Panel C), antibodies to desmosomal and hemidesmosomal proteins are associated with both acantholysis and suprabasal and subepidermal clefting and produce a combined pattern of staining on immunofluorescence.

 
Direct immunofluorescence staining with use of the patient's skin as a substrate and fluorescein-conjugated antibodies directed against human immunoglobulins and complement revealed IgG and C3 both along the epidermal basement zone and between the keratinocytes of the lower epidermis, in an intercellular, netlike pattern (Figure 2C). Indirect immunofluorescence studies of dilutions of the patient's serum applied to a monkey-esophagus substrate revealed circulating antibodies that bound to the intercellular regions of the epithelium at a titer of 1:80.

The proteins targeted by autoantibodies in pemphigus are localized to the desmosomes; in pemphigoid, they are localized to the hemidesmosomes; and in paraneoplastic pemphigus, they are localized to both (Figure 3). The binding of antibody to these proteins is associated with acantholysis, detachment of cells from the basement membrane, or both these processes. Desmogleins 1 and 3, which are targeted in pemphigus, are found only in desmosomes.16 In contrast, the two antigens targeted in pemphigoid are present only in hemidesmosomes: bullous pemphigoid antigen 1 is an intracellular desmoplakin component of the hemidesmosome, and bullous pemphigoid antigen 2 is a unique transmembrane protein that interacts with hemidesmosomes and the basement membrane.17 Antibodies to both desmogleins 1 and 3 and bullous pemphigoid antigen 2 are present in paraneoplastic pemphigus. Other plakins targeted by autoantibodies in paraneoplastic pemphigus are components of both desmosomes and hemidesmosomes (Table 3).18 The antibodies and other immunoreactants are visualized in situ by staining for human IgG and C3. In paraneoplastic pemphigus, IgG and C3 are deposited in an intercellular netlike pattern corresponding to desmosomes (in a manner similar to that in pemphigus) and also along the epidermal basement-membrane zone, corresponding to hemidesmosomes (in a manner similar to that in pemphigoid). The presence of staining in both of these distributions is characteristic of paraneoplastic pemphigus and distinguishes it from both pemphigus and pemphigoid.

Since the combination of clinical, pathological, and immunopathological findings in this case is characteristic of paraneoplastic pemphigus and since recent studies have not shown a correlation between the serum autoantibody profile and the clinical phenotype,19 immunoprecipitation studies were not performed in this patient.

No cases specifically classified as MALT lymphoma have been reported in association with paraneoplastic pemphigus. In most reports, however, the lymphomas are not well characterized, and it is possible that some of them may be MALT lymphomas.

Dr. McCluskey: Dr. Ryan, would you describe this patient's follow-up?

Dr. Ryan: The patient was initially treated with corticosteroids and mycophenolate mofetil, with only modest improvement in the lesions on the skin and virtually no improvement in the mucosal lesions. We decided to add weekly treatment with anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody (rituximab) to attempt to control the B-cell lymphoma, since there are reports of patients with paraneoplastic pemphigus and B-cell lymphoma who had improvement with this treatment.20,21 After two months of therapy, she had tremendous improvement in her skin lesions and moderate improvement in her oral lesions. Unfortunately, five months after presentation, she died suddenly while in a rehabilitation facility. An autopsy was not performed.

Anatomical Diagnosis

Paraneoplastic pemphigus associated with gastric marginal-zone B-cell lymphoma of mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT lymphoma).
