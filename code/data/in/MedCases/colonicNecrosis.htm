Case 37-2003 — A 79-Year-Old Man with Coronary Artery Disease, Peripheral Vascular Disease, End-Stage Renal Disease, and Abdominal Pain and Distention

Presentation of Case

A 79-year-old man was admitted to the hospital because of occlusion at a vascular access site in the right forearm for hemodialysis and worsening intermittent claudication.

There was a history of hypertension and coronary artery disease, and the patient had had an acute anterior myocardial infarction six years before this admission, for which he had received tissue plasminogen activator (alteplase); atheroemboli complicated the procedure, and intermittent claudication in both legs and end-stage renal disease developed. After four years of peritoneal dialysis (two years before the current admission), hemodialysis was initiated. Coronary-artery bypass grafting was performed three months after the infarction occurred, because there was three-vessel coronary disease. Cardiac ultrasonography performed 16 months before admission revealed inferior and apical akinesis, diastolic dysfunction, and thickening of the upper septum.

Two weeks before the current admission, the intermittent claudication became more severe, and the leg pain was present even when the patient was at rest. Two days before the current admission, he returned to the hospital from a hemodialysis facility because of poor flow from an arteriovenous fistula that had been constructed 32 months previously and that had required multiple thrombectomies and thromboplasties. After assessment, he was sent home with an appointment at the hospital two days later for a study with radiographic contrast agent and another hemodialysis session.

The patient had had paroxysmal atrial fibrillation, ventricular tachycardia, and peptic ulcer disease in the past. For 60 years, he had smoked up to two packs of cigarettes daily; he had discontinued smoking several years before admission because of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease and bronchospasm. A left upper lobectomy for cancer was performed four and a half years before admission. The patient drank little alcohol, and he did not have diabetes. His medications were albuterol, ipratropium, and triamcinolone acetonide inhalers; warfarin; metoprolol; simvastatin; and aspirin (81 mg daily).

At the time of the patient's admission, the temperature was 37°C, the pulse was 75 beats per minute, and the respiratory rate was 20 breaths per minute. The blood pressure was 120/70 mm Hg. The patient weighed 82 kg. No rash or lymphadenopathy was found. The jugular venous pressure was 5 cm of blood. The carotid pulse and the pulse in the arm were normal and without bruits, and the hands were well perfused. The femoral, popliteal, dorsalis pedis, and posterior tibial pulses were absent bilaterally; the feet were marginally perfused. The arteriovenous fistula was thrombosed. Diffuse expiratory wheezes were heard in both lungs. A grade 1 systolic cardiac murmur was present along the left sternal border. Examination of the abdomen and neurologic evaluation were unremarkable.

Laboratory tests were performed (Table 1 and Table 2). An electrocardiogram revealed a normal rhythm with ST-segment depressions and poor R-wave progression. A fistulographic study confirmed the presence of multiple areas of severe thrombosis within the right-forearm graft. Several attempts at angioplasty produced satisfactory flow. The patient received oxygen by cannula, his customary inhalers, docusate sodium, simvastatin, metoprolol, aspirin, and oxycodone–acetaminophen for pain. The temperature rose once to 37.4°C and was otherwise normal. The patient wheezed frequently.

On the second hospital day, laboratory tests were again performed (Table 2). An intravenous digital-subtraction angiogram of the aortic–pelvic region (Figure 1) disclosed diffuse atherosclerotic disease throughout the aortoiliac system, but the common and external iliac and common femoral arteries were patent bilaterally, as were the right deep and superficial femoral arteries; there was occlusion of the left superficial femoral artery at its origin, with reconstitution distally by the deep femoral artery. Later that day, the patient reported chest pain, and atrial fibrillation was observed; digoxin was given (0.75 mg intravenously), and the rhythm reverted to normal overnight. Transthoracic cardiac ultrasonography showed a left ventricle of normal size and normal systolic function, but there was segmental left ventricular dysfunction, with akinetic areas at the anterior apex and midportion, along with shallow aneurysmal bulging; the estimated ejection fraction was 59 percent.

On the third day, the patient had nausea and vomiting, but no abdominal pain. The jugular venous pressure was 7 cm of blood, and scattered wheezes were present. A stool specimen was positive for occult blood. Laboratory tests were performed (Table 1 and Table 2). A chest radiograph revealed mild linear atelectasis or scarring in the middle and lower zones of the right lung; the pulmonary vascularity was mildly prominent; no pneumonia was detected; the cardiac silhouette was normal. Two oral doses of sodium polystyrene sulfonate in sorbitol (Kayexalate, 60 g each) were given, one that afternoon and the other that night.

On the fourth hospital day, the levels of vitamin B12 and folic acid were normal. The results of other laboratory tests are shown in Table 1 and Table 2. Hemodialysis was resumed, and 2 units of packed red cells were transfused. A gastroenterologic consultant found the abdomen distended, with normal bowel sounds and no tenderness, and he elected to delay endoscopic examination. Radiography of the abdomen (Figure 2) performed with the patient in the supine position revealed marked dilatation of both large and small bowel loops, consistent with ileus; there was no asymmetry to suggest obstruction, and the outlines of the organs on imaging were unremarkable. Another chest radiograph disclosed clear lungs and no subphrenic gas.

On the fifth hospital day, the patient had no chest or abdominal pain, but his abdomen was more distended and diffusely tender on gentle palpation, with diminished bowel sounds and mild rebound tenderness. He remained afebrile, with bilateral wheezes and no crackles. Laboratory tests were performed (Table 1 and Table 2). On the sixth hospital day, the patient had abdominal pain. The abdomen remained distended and tender, with occasional bowel sounds. Laboratory tests were performed (Table 1 and Table 2). Hemodialysis was performed. The patient was unwilling to have a nasogastric tube placed or to undergo flexible sigmoidoscopy. No oral intake was permitted. Radiographs of the abdomen, performed with the patient in the supine and upright positions, revealed considerable gas throughout the colon and a dilated cecum; there was a paucity of gas in the small bowel, which was believed to have resulted from colonic ileus rather than from bowel obstruction.

On the seventh hospital day, the patient again had abdominal pain and passed two diarrheal stools that were positive for occult blood. The temperature was 36.8°C. The abdomen remained distended and tender, with infrequent bowel sounds. Laboratory tests were performed (Table 1 and Table 2). Abdominal–pelvic CT was repeated (Figure 4), with oral and intravenous administration of contrast material. It showed a large amount of free intraperitoneal air anteriorly and smaller air bubbles in the fat and elsewhere in the middle and upper portions of the abdomen; oral contrast material flowed to the rectum without extravasation; there was minimal thickening of the ascending colon and cecum; sigmoid diverticula were present, without diverticulitis; and no site of perforation was identified.

Differential Diagnosis

Dr. Peter B. Kelsey: This 79-year-old man with a history of cholesterol emboli with renal failure and claudication entered the hospital for revision of an occluded atrioventricular shunt and evaluation of worsening claudication. Some event that occurred during the first 48 hours of his hospitalization resulted in progressive bowel dilatation over the next five days and finally in the appearance of intraperitoneal free air that was suggestive of bowel infarction and perforation.

I shall first examine the possible gastrointestinal consequences of this patient's medical history. Then I will examine the events that occurred in the hospital that could have triggered the ileus and consequent perforation.

May we see the radiographs please?

Dr. Steven Chen: Abdominal aortography was performed on the second hospital day (Figure 1). Because the patient lacked femoral pulses, venous access through the common femoral vein was used for the injection of contrast material. There was severe atherosclerotic disease throughout the aorta and the common iliac vessels. The superior mesenteric artery was patent.

On the fourth day, anteroposterior abdominal radiographs, obtained with the patient in the supine position (Figure 2), revealed marked, diffuse dilatation of the entire colon.

On the fifth hospital day, a CT scan of the abdomen (Figure 3), obtained after the oral and intravenous administration of contrast material, revealed dilatation of the large bowel without thickening of the wall. There was no evidence of an obstructing lesion. The mesenteric vessels (not shown) were all patent.

A CT scan of the abdomen obtained on the seventh day (Figure 4) showed a large amount of free intraperitoneal air and decompression of the cecum. These findings are consistent with colonic perforation, although the exact location of the perforation was not seen.

Dr. Kelsey: Given the medical history, three types of chronic injury to the bowel may have been present when the patient entered the hospital.

Preexisting Conditions

            Vascular Disease

First, there was a long history of atherosclerotic vascular disease. We can assume that there was atherosclerosis of the mesenteric arteries. However, the patient had no history of postprandial abdominal pain, malabsorption, or weight loss. Therefore, clinically significant mesenteric ischemia seems unlikely. Furthermore, the arteriogram and the CT scans reveal that the proximal superior mesenteric artery was largely spared. Could the patient have had mesenteric venous thrombosis resulting in chronic ischemia? The diagnosis of mesenteric venous thrombosis can be difficult to make. Severe abdominal pain is often present, and the bowel wall is thickened. Pain did not develop in this patient until well into his hospital course, and the serial CT scans demonstrated no thickening of the bowel wall.1 In addition, the larger venous structures were well visualized and normal in appearance. Thus, we can rule out both large-vessel venous disease and arterial disease.

Was there vasculitis? There was no history of polyarthritis, hypertension, characteristic skin lesions such as purpura or rashes, or weight loss. We are not aware of previous gastrointestinal symptoms such as fluctuating abdominal pain or intermittent intestinal bleeding. Thus, polyarteritis nodosa, Henoch–Schönlein purpura, and systemic lupus erythematosus seem unlikely. There was no history of asthma, eosinophilia, or positivity for antineutrophil cytoplasmic autoantibodies to suggest the Churg–Strauss syndrome or Wegener's granulomatosis. There were no typical skin lesions, abnormalities on liver-function tests, hepatomegaly, or history of hepatitis C virus, so cryoglobulinemia is not likely.2 Thus, vasculitis seems an unlikely cause of this patient's problem.

            Atheroemboli

The second type of chronic bowel injury that might be present in this patient is related to his history of atheroemboli, a recognized complication of his previous therapy with tissue plasminogen activator.3 In 30 percent of patients who have renal failure after cholesterol emboli, there is evidence of gastrointestinal involvement.4 However, I suspect that subclinical injury may be present much more frequently and that careful pathological examination of this man's small bowel and colon would disclose areas of scarring related to this earlier event. How long after a shower of cholesterol emboli can clinical events continue to evolve? Renal function can continue to decline for eight weeks after the initial insult,4 and cholesterol crystals continue to be found in tissues six months after the event.5 Perforation of the small bowel has been reported up to four months after angiography.6,7 Anticoagulant therapy with heparin or warfarin may potentiate the further shedding of atheromatous tissue into the bloodstream.8 The inflammatory lesions caused by the cholesterol shards probably subside within a year after the initial event, but areas of scarring might progress to necrosis of the full thickness of the bowel wall when it is further stressed by a second ischemic event.

            Amyloidosis

The third type of gastrointestinal injury relates to the possible consequences of long-term peritoneal dialysis, hemodialysis, or chronic renal failure. Fifty percent of patients who undergo peritoneal dialysis for four years or longer have microscopical amyloid deposition of the beta2-microglobulin type in joints and bones and, less frequently, in the gastrointestinal mucosa, the liver, and the spleen. Clinically significant disease, however, requires the use of dialysis for longer than 10 years.9,10 Colonic involvement after many years of peritoneal dialysis occasionally leads to progressive thickening of the bowel wall, pseudo-obstruction, bacterial overgrowth, and constipation. Perforation is a rare complication.11 Our patient's period of dialysis was too short, he had none of these clinical findings, and the bowel-wall thickness was normal — all of which rule out an infiltrative process such as amyloid deposition. What in-hospital exposures or events, then, could have triggered the ileus and subsequent perforation?

Exposures and Changes during Hospitalization

            Drugs and Metabolic Abnormalities

Although a number of drugs are associated with the development of ileus, such as anticholinergic agents, calcium-channel blockers, and phenothiazines, opiates lead the list of in-hospital offenders. The doses of oxycodone the patient received after his arteriovenous-graft thrombectomy could have led to this degree of ileus. No metabolic abnormalities such as hyponatremia, hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia, or hypermagnesemia were noted.

            Infection and Inflammation

Inflammation from an intraabdominal, intrathoracic, or systemic source could lead to ileus. The patient did not have sufficiently severe diarrhea or hematochezia to suggest an enteric infection such as Clostridium difficile or Escherichia coli O157:H7. His first abdominal CT scan demonstrated no stranding, mass, or evidence of a localized perforation from a duodenal ulcer, tumor, or diverticulum. There was no evidence of appendicitis, pancreatitis, cholecystitis, perinephric abscess, or retroperitoneal hematoma. Multiple radiographs of the chest showed no thoracic inflammatory process. Finally, there were no signs of a systemic infection such as endocarditis.

            Circulatory Problems

Sudden decreases in perfusion pressure after an embolic event, changes in cardiac output, or shock can also result in ileus. Nothing in the case record suggested that there was a prolonged episode of hypotension during the period of atrial fibrillation. Could thrombus from the left ventricular aneurysm have embolized to both the coronary ostia and the mesentery? The echocardiogram did demonstrate apical akinesis with a shallow aneurysmal dilatation, but no clot was seen. In addition, there was no left ventricular hypertrophy, marked enlargement of the left atrium, or mitral stenosis. Finally, the patient received adequate anticoagulation throughout this period. Thus, his overall risk of a thromboembolic event was not high, unless he had a superimposed hypercoagulable disorder. He had required multiple thrombectomies and thromboplasties, but this difficulty occurs in more than 30 percent of patients with atrioventricular fistulas.

Could the patient have had an embolic event after the balloon angioplasty of his atrioventricular fistula? Systemic emboli could occur in this scenario only through a right-to-left shunt such as a patent foramen ovale, and this defect was not seen on careful review of the two echocardiograms. The patient had undergone multiple thromboplasties in the past, and no embolic events had been mentioned. If he did have embolization to the mesentery, there was no evidence of the characteristic clinical and radiologic features that are seen in more than 50 percent of patients with acute mesenteric ischemia, such as pain or bowel-wall thickening.

Did something occur at the time of intravenous digital-subtraction angiography? There are no reports in the literature of bowel complications after this procedure. However, if this procedure is chosen only after multiple failed attempts at establishing arterial access, atheroemboli become a real threat.

At the time of increasing abdominal distention, it might have been appropriate to perform a sigmoidoscopy with minimal air insufflation in order to look for ischemia and to rule out obstruction of the lower tract. Neostigmine, injected intravenously in a dose of 2.0 mg, has been used successfully to treat Ogilvie's syndrome due to opiate use or in the postoperative setting.4 It must be used with caution during the postinfarction period. If there was mucosal injury, a biopsy might yield a specific diagnosis.

Summary

So what happened here? After thromboplasty, the patient received several doses of oxycodone, resulting in decreased bowel motility. I believe that vascular manipulation during either the thromboplasty or the angiography resulted in a shower of atheroemboli that traveled to his mesenteric circulation. The patient's legs were spared further injury because of their already compromised circulation. His intestine, which was previously scarred, dilated further, and eventually, ischemic perforation occurred. The diagnostic procedure was urgent surgical exploration, which I would expect to reveal areas of patchy ischemia and cholesterol clefts in the small bowel and colon.

Dr. Robert T. McCluskey (Pathology): Dr. Bazari, would you comment on your impressions of this patient?

Dr. Hasan Bazari (Nephrology): It was clear that he had an acute abdomen, and my leading diagnosis was a low-flow state in a patient with diffuse vasculopathy and known atheroembolic disease. There were a number of confounding features, such as the use of opiates, that could have contributed to distention and a propensity to perforation.

Dr. Andrew Chan (Gastroenterology): At first, we believed that the patient's initial symptoms of nausea and abdominal distention were probably related to uremia. Because his blood counts were stable and he had numerous ongoing cardiac problems, we elected to postpone endoscopic examination. When his condition worsened, we became concerned about the possibility of mesenteric ischemia or cholesterol emboli. Although the initial CT angiographic study showed only a dilated colon, we doubted that ileus alone could explain his severe symptoms and the findings on examination. Because he represented a poor operative risk, we elected to observe him and to obtain a follow-up CT scan to evaluate for evidence of evolving bowel ischemia. The demonstration of perforation on this study prompted a surgical exploration.

Clinical Diagnosis

Ischemic necrosis with perforation of the colon, possibly caused by atheroemboli.

Dr. Peter B. Kelsey's Diagnoses

Opiate-induced ileus.

Focal ischemic injury to the small bowel and colon with perforation, possibly due to atheroemboli.

Pathological Discussion

Dr. Janey Pratt (Surgery): We performed an exploratory laparotomy. On entering the abdomen, we found a large amount of free air. The right and transverse colon were distended. There was a tiny linear tear in one of the tinea of the right colon. We performed a right ileocolectomy.

Dr. Gregory Y. Lauwers: There was a fibrinous exudate on the serosal surface of the colon and a 4.5-cm area of hemorrhagic, erythematous, and friable mucosa 8 cm from the distal margin of resection. The remaining mucosa was edematous but otherwise unremarkable.

Microscopical examination revealed areas of transmural necrosis surrounded by superficial and deep mucosal ulcerations (Figure 5A). Small, eosinophilic, angulated crystals were embedded in the mucosal ulcers (Figure 5B). Although the residual colonic mucosa was unremarkable, patchy submucosal fibrosis and atrophy of the muscularis propria were present, indicating the presence of chronic ischemia (Figure 5C). There was no evidence of recent vascular thrombosis or fibrin or cholesterol emboli. Our diagnosis was colonic necrosis due to sodium polystyrene sulfonate in sorbitol.

In 1987, Lillemoe et al.12 reported a series of five patients with uremia who had colonic necrosis that was temporally associated with the prescription of enemas of sodium polystyrene sulfonate in sorbitol. This rare complication has subsequently been well described.13,14,15,16,17 Gerstman et al.18 reported its occurrence in 0.27 percent of hospitalized patients and in 1.8 percent of patients who received sodium polystyrene sulfonate in sorbitol after surgery.

Most cases of colonic necrosis after the administration of this preparation are diagnosed within a period ranging from hours to several days after the last dose is given. The reported doses vary from 20 g in one day to 120 g in two days.13,14,15,16,17 The patients have abdominal pain, nausea, or bloody diarrhea.14,17 The histopathological findings vary from patchy mucosal erosions or ulcerations to pseudomembranes to transmural necrosis.17 The observation of angulated eosinophilic crystals of sodium polystyrene sulfonate in areas of ulceration suggests the diagnosis.14,17

Sodium polystyrene sulfonate is a cation-exchange resin that is administered orally or as an enema that has been used for the management of hyperkalemia since the 1970s. When administered orally, it first releases sodium ions in the acidic stomach in exchange for hydrogen ions that bind to the resin. In the intestines, the hydrogen ions are exchanged for potassium. Then, the bound potassium is excreted in the stool, thereby lowering the serum potassium level. The sodium polystyrene sulfonate is mixed with sorbitol, a hypertonic solution that has the role of a carthartic agent, to avoid constipation and fecal impaction.

It can be difficult to define the role of this preparation in the development of colonic necrosis in patients who present with multiple medical problems and are prone to ischemic disorders. Evidence of colonic necrosis in a mouse model as a result of its administration supports a direct toxic effect of this combination, as does the occurrence of upper gastrointestinal injury in patients with or without lower gastrointestinal complications.19,20

Sorbitol is believed to be responsible for changes in the hemodynamics of the bowel wall, but the mechanism of these changes remains unclear. The overall medical condition of the patient seems to be important. Many patients who have this complication are hypovolemic or hypotensive after surgery or dialysis or have peripheral vascular disease. The intestinal vasculature of patients with renal disorders may be prone to vasoconstriction because their angiotensin levels are increased as a result of elevated renin levels.21 Production of prostaglandins may be stimulated by sorbitol and could play a part in causing mucosal injury.22

Dr. Bazari: In the past year, I have seen two other patients in whom necrosis developed in the gastrointestinal tract after the administration of sodium polystyrene sulfonate in sorbitol and in whom crystals were evident in the affected area on histologic examination. One case involved the stomach, and the other the colon. I think we have to be aware that this preparation can have physiological consequences in certain patients. As in this case, toxic effects typically occur in patients with multiple medical problems. Perhaps we should not try so hard to avoid dialysis by using large amounts of this preparation.

The patient recovered uneventfully and was discharged on the 18th hospital day, but 18 months later he was readmitted with sepsis of the vascular access site and an ischemic leg. With his family present, the patient stated that he did not want amputation or other life-saving measures; he wanted to die on his own terms. We kept him comfortable, and he died shortly thereafter. An autopsy was not performed.

Anatomical Diagnoses

Colonic necrosis with perforation due to sodium polystyrene sulfonate in sorbitol.

Ischemic colitis, healed.
