Case 40-2003 — A 14-Month-Old Boy with Recurrent Abdominal Distention and Diarrhea

Presentation of Case

A 14-month-old boy was admitted to the hospital because of recurrent abdominal distention, vomiting, and diarrhea.

The boy had been born at another hospital at 34 weeks' gestation and weighed 2.56 kg at birth. He had been admitted to the newborn intensive care unit for three days, during which time he received antibiotics and had an unremarkable course. He was bottle-fed and tube-fed on his first day of life but was breast-fed beginning on the second day.

His mother had had ulcerative colitis for 22 years (since the age of 20 years); it was controlled with hydrocortisone in enema form. During the two years before this pregnancy, she had had flares that were managed with prednisone administered systemically. She consumed "organic" milk and cheese, which she believed to be pasteurized. Her most recent endoscopic examination, performed at another hospital, showed granulomatous lesions in the distal ileum.

The boy grew well despite having "colicky" reactions to breast-feeding. At seven months of age, his hemoglobin level was 7 g per deciliter, and iron was prescribed. He was otherwise well until nine months of age, when vomiting occurred and then resolved. Nine days after the resolution of the vomiting, diarrhea began. Stool cultures were sterile, and examination of the stool revealed no ova or parasites. He passed five to seven stools each day until the diarrhea resolved, two days later. Eight days after the resolution of the diarrhea, the hemoglobin level was 10 g per deciliter. Radiographs of the abdomen and an abdominal ultrasonographic image obtained elsewhere were said to be normal.

By the time the boy was 11 months of age (approximately 3 months before admission), he had lost 1.4 kg of body weight. After an intravenous infusion of fluid and electrolytes, his condition improved, except for intermittent bouts of foul-smelling diarrhea. About two and half months before admission, examination showed pallor and distention of the abdomen, with occasional high-pitched bowel sounds; no masses were palpated. The same day, an upper gastrointestinal series with a small-bowel follow-through study (Figure 1) revealed several massively dilated loops of distal small bowel with intervening areas of stricture. The results of a test for antigliadin antibodies and a sweat chloride test were negative, and the serum IgA level was normal.

Seven weeks before admission, the hematocrit was 24.0 percent, the white-cell count 8200 per cubic millimeter, and the platelet count 671,000 per cubic millimeter; the mean corpuscular volume was 61 µm3. The same day, an esophagogastroduodenoscopic examination showed that the esophagus, stomach, duodenal bulb, and second portion of the duodenum were normal. Biopsy specimens obtained from each location were normal, and a thiazine stain for Helicobacter pylori was negative; a duodenal-fluid aspirate contained no protozoal or helminthic ova. Colonoscopic examination showed discontinuous areas of ulcerated and bleeding mucosa, with friable mucosa in the rectum and cecum; the ileocecal valve was too ulcerated to be entered. There was nodularity, without ulceration, from the ascending colon to the sigmoid colon, most prominently in the transverse colon. Examination of a biopsy specimen taken from the cecum revealed an ulcer with a focal giant-cell reaction; examination of additional specimens taken from the ascending colon and rectum showed colonic mucosa with prominent lymphoid aggregates. Specimens from the transverse and sigmoid colon were normal.

Six weeks before admission, the boy's weight was 9.9 kg, and his length was 76.2 cm. Examination of the abdomen revealed no abnormalities except for slight distention. A week later, an abdominal pelvic computed tomographic (CT) scan showed extensive inflammatory changes in the right lower abdominal quadrant, medial to the cecum (Figure 2). There was abnormal enhancement of the cecal wall and ascending colon, with enlargement of the mesenteric lymph nodes, a few of which were calcified. There were several massively dilated loops of distal small bowel with air–fluid levels and intervening strictures. The other structures observed were normal.

One month before admission, laboratory tests were performed (Table 1). The boy's parents were instructed to give him a soft, mixed diet when symptoms were minor or absent. He continued to have bouts of abdominal distention approximately every 7 to 10 days, followed by episodes of diarrhea with foul-smelling green stools, vomiting, and apparent abdominal pain lasting approximately 36 hours and not accompanied by fever, hematochezia, or the passage of mucus. One week before admission, another abdominopelvic CT scan showed changes nearly identical to those seen on the previous scan, as well as a small amount of intraperitoneal fluid in the pelvis and diffuse hepatic fatty infiltration. During the two days before admission, his oral intake decreased, and he had persistent diarrhea, diminished urinary output, and increasing lethargy. He was admitted to this hospital.

The boy could walk, although he had walked infrequently in recent months. He was able to say "dada" but only babbled otherwise. No one in the family had a chronic cough or a history of tuberculosis.

The temperature was 37.5°C, the pulse was 135 beats per minute, and the respiratory rate was 28 breaths per minute. The boy's body length was 77.4 cm (just below the 50th percentile), and his weight was 10 kg (just below the 25th percentile). The blood pressure was 85/65 mm Hg.

On physical examination, the boy was alert and fussy. The lips and mucous membranes were dry, and there was no periorbital edema. The lungs and heart were normal. The abdomen was soft and was neither distended nor tender; the bowel sounds were normal. The liver and spleen were not felt. There was questionable peripheral nonpitting edema.

The urine was normal. Laboratory tests were performed (Table 1). The levels of glucose, conjugated and total bilirubin, magnesium, aspartate aminotransferase, alanine aminotransferase, and alkaline phosphatase were normal.

A diagnostic procedure was performed.

Differential Diagnosis

Dr. Benjamin D. Gold: May we review the radiologic studies?

Dr. Sjirk J. Westra: The small-bowel follow-through study (Figure 1) shows normal propagation of contrast material through the proximal small bowel and two areas of severe narrowing at the distal ileum, with markedly dilated loops of small bowel, indicating a high grade of obstruction. The abdominal CT scan (Figure 2) reveals dilated looping of the distal small bowel; inflammatory changes in the right lower quadrant, with pericecal thickening; and a calcified mesenteric lymph node. There is also thickening of the rectal wall.

Dr. Gold: This 14-month-old boy presented with chronic gastrointestinal symptoms, iron-deficiency anemia, elevated levels of acute-phase reactants, and radiologically and endoscopically documented disease of the colon and distal small bowel, which contained evidence of strictures. A biopsy specimen showed chronic inflammation, with a giant-cell reaction.

I have grouped the possible differential diagnoses into four main categories (Table 2): gastrointestinal diseases, hematologic diseases, systemic inflammatory diseases, and infectious diseases. Among the gastrointestinal diseases, inflammatory bowel disease — specifically, Crohn's disease1,2,3 and ulcerative colitis (which is a less likely possibility) — are the only important candidates in this case. On the basis of the endoscopic findings alone, nodular lymphoid hyperplasia deserves inclusion in the differential diagnosis, but it is probably not the definitive lesion that resulted in the illnesses in this patient's history and his current condition.4,5 The other gastrointestinal disorders listed in Table 2 are also unlikely to be the cause of this patient's illness.

I believe that the child's hematologic abnormalities are secondary. The microcytic hypochromic anemia is most likely due to both a primary nutritional deficiency and gastrointestinal blood loss.6 Other hematologic disorders are unlikely to explain this patient's clinical problems.

Among the systemic inflammatory processes, sarcoidosis should be strongly considered as a granulomatous disease that can occasionally involve the gastrointestinal tract.7,8,9,10 The vasculitides — in particular, juvenile dermatomyositis and polyarteritis nodosa11,12,13,14 — should also be considered. The other disorders listed in Table 2 are less probable causes of this patient's illness.

Of the infectious diseases, the most likely possibilities are intestinal tuberculosis15,16 and yersinia infection17,18,19 — specifically, infection with Yersinia enterocolitica or Y. pseudotuberculosis. I will focus on the four entities that I believe are the most important to consider in the differential diagnosis, beginning with the least likely.

Yersinia Infection

One of the possibilities that should be considered is infection with yersinia, a gram-negative bacillus17 — specifically, either Y. enterocolitica or Y. pseudotuberculosis. The CT findings in this child are consistent with those described previously in children with yersinia infection.17,18,20 Consumption of contaminated food, water, or milk is often implicated in the transmission of yersinia infection.19 The dietary history of the boy's mother includes the consumption of organic milk and cheese, which might support an infection with this organism, although she did not recall consuming unpasteurized products.

Disease due to yersinia infection is usually self-limited, but it can be life-threatening if the infection is systemic.17 Septicemia can occur in both immunocompetent and immunocompromised hosts. Patients less than three months of age typically have fever associated with abdominal distention, as well as bloody diarrhea (which this child did not have); vomiting is less common. The duration of the illness and the other clinical findings in this case would be unusual for yersinia infection, and thus I do not believe that such infection is the diagnosis in this case.

Sarcoidosis

The third most likely diagnosis on my list is infantile sarcoidosis. The incidence of sarcoidosis has two peaks: one in men and women 25 to 35 years of age and the other in women 45 to 65 years of age.8,21 Almost all children with this disease have fever, weight loss, and fatigue9,10; peripheral lymphadenopathy is present in 67 percent, and skin lesions, which this child did not have, are usually macular and papular. Both children and adults may present with gastrointestinal manifestations. These include gastrointestinal ulcerations with noncaseating granulomas, sometimes with skip lesions. The liver may also be involved.22,23 More than 60 percent of patients have mesenteric lymphadenopathy, which was observed in this case, and in children with gastrointestinal sarcoidosis, there is often no lung disease.7,8,9,10

There are two types of childhood sarcoidosis. The late type occurs in children 8 to 15 years of age and involves clinical features similar to those in adults, with the triad of uveitis, arthritis, and skin lesions; pulmonary disease is not present initially.7,9 The second type, which has a very early onset, is called the Blau syndrome.8,10 This disorder has an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern and has been linked to chromosome 16. Patients have granulomatous arthritis, iritis, a rash, polyclonal hyperglobulinemia with circulating immune complexes, depressed cellular immunity, and cutaneous anergy; there is no pulmonary involvement. The child in the case under discussion does not have the systemic manifestations of the Blau syndrome, and there is no family history suggesting autosomal dominant inheritance of such a syndrome. Thus, although it merits serious consideration, I do not believe the diagnosis in this case is infantile sarcoidosis.

Crohn's Disease

The second choice on my list of possible diagnoses in this case is Crohn's disease.24,25 In Crohn's disease, which can involve any segment of the gastrointestinal tract, there may be transmural inflammation with skip lesions as well as strictures, which have been described in both adults and children.2,26,27 Crohn's disease can be focal and asymmetric, and there may be microscopic noncaseating granulomas and perianal disease with fissures, fistulas, or both. Crohn's disease has extraintestinal manifestations in 25 percent or more of those who are affected.28 This child did not have involvement of the areas most commonly described in patients with Crohn's disease, such as the skin (in the form of erythema nodosum or pyoderma gangrenosum), mouth, joints, or eyes.

Although population-based studies are lacking, it has been estimated that during the year 2000, Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis affected 1 million to 2 million Americans at any given time; only 25 percent of the cases were diagnosed before the age of 20 years.29 Crohn's disease is seen in 1 to 10 of every 100,000 adults and in approximately 2 to 4 of every 100,000 children; ulcerative colitis affects 5 to 18 of every 100,000 adults and 2 to 4 of every 100,000 children.24,25,26,27,28,29,30,31 A bimodal age distribution has been described, and the incidence of the disease during infancy seems to be increasing.26

It is difficult to rule out Crohn's disease in this case because of this evidence of its increasingly early onset and because of the maternal history of granulomatous ileitis. However, because of the very young age of this child at his initial presentation, the presence of vomiting (which is unusual in Crohn's disease), and the location of the disease, I would be concerned about another cause.

Tuberculosis

Finally, I would like to consider the possibility of gastrointestinal tuberculosis. Patients who have gastrointestinal tuberculosis may present with a fulminant process characterized by rapid onset and sepsis, with central nervous system involvement, or they may present with a chronic, waxing and waning process characterized by both constitutional and localized signs and symptoms. The signs and symptoms include abdominal pain, vomiting, and diarrhea that may or may not be bloody. On imaging studies and at colonoscopy, transverse or circumferential ulcerations are observed, as are nodularity, deformity of the ileocecal valve, strictures, pouch formation, and fibrous bands.32,33,34 Aphthous ulcers and multiple erosions may also be seen.35

Epidemiologic data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention show an overall decline in reported cases of tuberculosis in the United States during the past 20 years.36,37,38,39 Only 6 percent of the cases involve persons less than 15 years of age37,39; the majority occur in persons 25 to 44 years of age. The prevalence of tuberculosis among persons less than 15 years old does not vary according to race, but in older age groups white and Hispanic persons predominate. Multidrug-resistant strains are more common in nonwhite persons.40,41 From 1991 to 2001, there was a decline in cases among persons born in the United States but a gradual increase in cases among persons born elsewhere.37,39

On the basis of the constellation of this child's signs and symptoms, his age at the initial presentation, and the absence of features that would support other diagnoses, I conclude that the diagnosis is intestinal tuberculosis. My diagnostic test of choice would be a full-thickness biopsy of the ileum or cecum or the liver, with cultures and stains for Mycobacterium tuberculosis and other pathogens.

Dr. Nancy Lee Harris (Pathology): Dr. Harland Winter cared for this patient. Would you give us your impressions?

Dr. Harland S. Winter (Pediatric Gastroenterology): The illnesses affecting this patient and his mother were an enigma to us. We thought that the mother's inflammatory bowel disease probably was not Crohn's disease. We considered infectious diseases in both the child and his mother, but neither appeared to be chronically ill. The mother had no history or evidence of cough or pulmonary disease that suggested tuberculosis. The ulcer in the child's cecum was consistent with the presence of Crohn's disease; however, the severity of the narrowing in the ileum and the relatively mild symptoms cast doubt on that diagnosis. Dr. Daniel Doody, a pediatric surgeon, and I discussed surgical intervention with the parents, who initially decided to wait before taking such a step. My working diagnosis was atypical necrotizing enterocolitis with a stricture in the ileum, associated with a previous ischemic event.

Pathological Discussion

Dr. Daniel P. Doody (Pediatric Surgery): Because the child did not show signs of improvement after four to six weeks and because the radiographic features were so impressive, we believed that surgery was indicated. Surgical exploration was performed through a transverse infraumbilical incision. There was a small amount of thin, serosanguineous peritoneal fluid, which was sent for culture. What was impressive, however, was the terminal ileum: this segment showed marked thickening, creeping fat, and subserosal studding — findings consistent with the presence of granulomas. These findings are also consistent with those I have seen in children with terminal ileitis due to inflammatory bowel disease. We explored the rest of the abdomen. The liver appeared normal, and a biopsy was not performed. Parts of the small intestine and the sigmoid colon showed some creeping fat with subserosal studding, but no evidence of stricture. We performed an ileocecal resection. The child had an uneventful postoperative course.

Dr. Fiona M. Graeme-Cook: The specimen consisted of 3.5 cm of terminal ileum, 4 cm of cecum, and an appendix 4.5 cm in length. The terminal ileum was markedly dilated, with a diameter at the proximal margin of 3 cm; it narrowed abruptly into a stricture 1 cm long, just proximal to the ileocecal valve. The entire region of the ileocecal valve was severely scarred with serosal fibrosis; multiple tan-to-white, rubbery nodules that were 1 to 2 mm in diameter studded the serosal surface. There was abnormal mucosa overlying the stricture and extending into the cecum.

Microscopical examination revealed an ulcer with a fissure that extended into the muscularis propria (Figure 3A). Multiple well-formed epithelioid granulomas were present within the bowel wall and on the serosal surface (Figure 3B), corresponding to the nodules described by Dr. Doody. The granulomas contained Langhans' giant cells and central areas of necrosis (Figure 3C). Numerous acid-fast bacilli were identified on Ziehl–Neelsen staining (Figure 3D). Discontinuous areas of granulomatous inflammation, often surrounded by hyperplastic lymphoid tissue, were present within the mucosa, corresponding to the endoscopic appearance of lymphoid hyperplasia.

Cultures of the specimen subsequently yielded M. tuberculosis. The final diagnosis was tuberculous enterocolitis.

Dr. Harris: Dr. Ella Griffiths reexamined the ileocecal-biopsy specimens obtained from the mother during her most recent postpartum surveillance endoscopic examination.

Dr. Ella Griffiths (Pathology, Mount Auburn Hospital): I reviewed the ileocecal specimens after the child was given the diagnosis of tuberculous enterocolitis and noted multiple granulomas and an area of ulceration and granulation tissue. Repeated Ziehl–Neelsen staining was negative. Polymerase-chain-reaction analysis was performed in an effort to identify M. tuberculosis, but DNA could not be amplified from the remaining tissue block.

Dr. Harris: I would like to ask Dr. Mark Pasternack to comment on the subsequent care of this patient.

Dr. Mark S. Pasternack (Pediatric Infectious Disease): Initially, we were concerned that the child's illness might be an atypical mycobacterial infection. He was treated with a combination of isoniazid, rifampin, ethambutol, and clarithromycin so that there would be coverage both for conventional M. tuberculosis infection and for M. avium infection. After we learned that the infectious agent was M. tuberculosis, the clarithromycin was stopped, and pyrazinamide was added to his therapeutic regimen. He received conventional four-drug therapy for two months, after which his treatment was completed with just isoniazid and pyrazinamide. Except for some diarrhea, he did remarkably well and thrived in every way.

Dr. Harris: Because of the child's diagnosis of tuberculous enterocolitis, his mother was examined. Dr. Basgoz, would you comment?

Dr. Nesli Basgoz (Infectious Disease): The patient's mother had traveled extensively in the developing world during her 20s, though she had not had clinically evident tuberculosis. We performed a test for tuberculosis with purified protein derivative; the result was positive, whereas the child's father had a negative test result. The mother was completely well, and the results of a physical examination were normal. I ordered a plain film radiograph of the chest and, subsequently, a CT scan.

Dr. Westra: The plain film (Figure 4A) revealed a reticulonodular interstitial infiltrate in the apex of the right lung. This finding was further evaluated by high-resolution CT scanning (Figure 4B). The CT scan showed a "tree-in-bud" appearance, which is characteristic of endobronchial spread of tuberculosis. There was also blunting of the right lateral costophrenic sulcus and scarring at the right lung base, probably indicating prior tuberculous pleuritis.

Dr. Basgoz: I was paged by the radiologist when the plain film of the chest was read, and we obtained induced sputum for smears and culture for mycobacteria. The smears were negative for mycobacteria, but the cultures grew the same organism as the child's cultures had grown. Cultures of breast milk and stool specimens were negative. We treated the mother with isoniazid, rifampin, ethambutol, pyrazinamide, and pyridoxine. Because of the duration of her infection, which we assumed dated from the time her granulomatous ileitis was diagnosed, and the presence of an underlying disease, we planned to extend her therapy for more than six months. However, atypical late, drug-induced hepatitis developed, so after seven months, the isoniazid and rifampin were discontinued. She continues to feel well.

We believe that this woman's current illness represents reactivation of tuberculosis acquired during her travels, in the setting of increased corticosteroid use for her inflammatory bowel disease. We assume that her child acquired the infection from her respiratory droplets, possibly by the oral route, with localization of the infection to the ileocecal region. This case highlights the fact that tuberculosis is opportunistic in patients with other underlying diseases and that it is important to obtain a travel history in all patients with suspected infectious diseases.

Dr. Harris: Dr. Rosaline Barron has followed the mother for inflammatory bowel disease. Would you comment on your assessment and follow-up of the patient's mother?

Dr. Rosaline F. Barron (Gastroenterology, Mount Auburn Hospital): The patient's mother had a 20-year history of ulcerative colitis. Colonoscopic examinations over the years had documented pancolitis but never ileal disease. The first postpartum surveillance colonoscopic examination, performed while she was asymptomatic, revealed a striking change: the development of ulcerated masses in the ileum and cecum and an ulcer on the ileocecal valve. The biopsy specimens contained noncaseating granulomas. CT scanning and a small-bowel series showed ileal thickening, which was thought to be consistent with the presence of Crohn's ileitis.

I was perplexed that dramatic ileal disease would suddenly develop in a patient with a 20-year history of typical ulcerative colitis while she was asymptomatic, so I asked the patient to consult another gastroenterologist. He concurred that her illness had probably been Crohn's disease all along. It was then that her son's illness unfolded and the diagnosis of tuberculous enteritis was made. After treatment with the antituberculous regimen, follow-up colonoscopic examination showed dramatic healing of the ileal and cecal ulcers and only mild stenosis of the terminal ileum.

Thus, this patient had asymptomatic tuberculous enteritis complicating long-standing ulcerative colitis. She had never received infliximab,42 which has been associated with reactivation of tuberculosis. The lesson is that in this age of globalization and world travel, tuberculosis remains a menace, especially to patients with immunosuppression, even when it is intermittent and moderate.

Anatomical Diagnosis

Tuberculous enterocolitis.
