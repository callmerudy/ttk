Case 24-2003 — A 10-Year-Old Girl with Recurrent Bouts of Abdominal Pain
Dawn M. McGee, M.D., Susan A. Connolly, M.D., and Robert H. Young, M.D.

Presentation of Case

A 10-year-old girl was admitted to the hospital because of abdominal pain, vomiting, and fever.

She had been in excellent health until about 18 months earlier, when she had the first of several bouts of severe pain in the right lower quadrant; each episode of pain persisted for an hour or two, was sometimes followed by nausea and vomiting, and then resolved. Six months before admission, she was taken to another hospital because of pain in the right flank. The results of an abdominal ultrasonographic study were unremarkable.

One month later, after another episode of abdominal pain, the girl was brought to this hospital. She had been born of a full-term pregnancy and had been delivered by cesarean section because of maternal hypertension. There was no history of irregular bowel function, weight loss, previous hospital admission, allergies, migraine, or joint pain. Her mother and a maternal uncle had diabetes mellitus; two older siblings were well. There was no family history of Crohn's disease, celiac disease, irritable bowel syndrome, lactose intolerance, autoimmune disease, or liver or thyroid problems.

The patient was afebrile and free of pain on arrival. A physical examination, including rectal examination, revealed no abnormalities. A stool specimen was negative for occult blood. Laboratory tests were performed (Table 1). An upper gastrointestinal and small-bowel series showed abundant fecal material throughout the colon. The esophagus and stomach were normal, and a water siphon test induced a tiny amount of gastroesophageal reflux. The duodenal bulb and sweep, small bowel loops, and transit time were normal. A breath test for Helicobacter pylori and examination of a stool specimen for Giardia lamblia were negative. Within the following month, a diagnosis of type 1 diabetes mellitus was confirmed; it was controlled with insulin (a neutral protamine Hagedorn preparation) and insulin lispro.

One month before admission, pain began to occur every day or two, anywhere in the abdomen, and in some instances radiated to the back or to either inguinal region. On occasion, vomiting accompanied the pain. The patient reported constipation without hematochezia. Laxatives were advised. There was no rash. During the same month, the blood glucose levels rose to 200 to 300 mg per deciliter (11.1 to 16.7 mmol per liter) and higher.

One week before admission, crampy abdominal pain without vomiting recurred, and a prolonged episode was evaluated at another hospital. The results of urinalysis were unremarkable, the blood count was normal, and a radiograph of the abdomen disclosed only stool and a considerable amount of gas in the bowel. Four days before admission, the patient returned to this hospital for follow-up examination. The findings on physical examination were unchanged. A single radiograph of the abdomen showed a moderate amount of stool in the colon. The pattern of gas in the bowel was unremarkable, the small bowel was not distended, and no free intraperitoneal air was detected. Laxatives were again advised. Four days later, the abdominal pain returned and worsened. The patient vomited a few times and became febrile. She was brought back to this hospital.

The temperature was 37.8°C, the pulse 110 beats per minute, and the respiratory rate 24 breaths per minute. The blood pressure was 105/70 mm Hg. The patient's height was 141 cm (75th to 90th percentile) and her weight 30.1 kg (50th to 75th percentile). On physical examination, she appeared pale and apprehensive. No lymphadenopathy, petechiae, or bruises were noted. The lungs and heart were normal. The abdomen was soft but tender throughout, with voluntary guarding, especially in the suprapubic region and right lower quadrant. The liver and spleen were not felt, no definite mass was detected, and there was no rebound tenderness. Findings on rectal examination were unremarkable; a stool specimen was negative for occult blood. The urine was positive (++) for glucose and ketones and trace-positive for protein; the sediment was unremarkable. Laboratory values are shown in Table 1 and Table 2.

A radiograph of the abdomen (Figure 1) showed slightly dilated loops of small bowel, without colonic gas, and a soft-tissue mass arising from the pelvis. An ultrasonographic examination of the abdomen and pelvis revealed a moderate amount of ascites in the pelvis. A large, complex mass extended from the level of the umbilicus to the bladder and occupied both the left and right lower quadrants of the abdomen and nearly the entire pelvis, displacing the bowel loops from the pelvis. The mass was primarily echogenic but contained multiple radiolucent areas, each slightly less than 1 cm in diameter. Color Doppler examination showed that the mass was not highly vascular, although a few vessels contained flowing blood. The liver, spleen, pancreas, and kidneys were normal.

The right ovary was not identified, and the right ovarian vein and broad ligament extended to the mass, strongly suggesting that the mass originated from the right ovary. A magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) study of the pelvis (Figure 3) revealed a heterogeneous mass (15 cm in vertical dimension, 7 cm in anteroposterior dimension, and 10 cm in transverse dimension) that arose from the pelvis; it was predominantly midline but extended to the left of midline. It had a bilobate configuration and contained multiple small, cystic areas that were thought to be ovarian follicles. On T1-weighted images, there were several areas of high signal intensity that appeared on T2-weighted images as dark areas and suggested the presence of hemorrhages within the mass. No calcification or fat was observed within the mass. The right ovary was not identified and was suspected to be involved with the mass. The left ovary, 4.2 by 2.5 by 2.3 cm, was displaced posteriorly by the mass and contained multiple small follicles. A moderate amount of ascites was observed. The right ureter was slightly dilated to the level of the mass; the kidneys were not included in the image field. The configuration of the uterus was prepubertal. A large part of the mass was not enhanced after the intravenous injection of gadolinium, suggesting the presence of torsion.

Differential Diagnosis

Dr. Dawn M. McGee: May we review the radiologic studies?

Dr. Susan A. Connolly: The first plain film of the kidneys, ureters, and bladder, obtained four days before admission, showed a normal bowel-gas pattern with a moderate amount of stool within the colon. A repeated radiograph obtained on admission (Figure 1) shows a soft-tissue mass in the pelvis as well as several dilated loops of small bowel, suggesting that the small bowel was at least partially obstructed. An ultrasonographic study of the abdomen confirmed the presence of a large pelvic mass and pelvic ascites. The mass was heterogeneous in echotexture, and there were multiple small, peripheral cysts. Doppler examination of the mass revealed minimal blood flow. The mass was thought to arise from the right adnexa, since a normal right ovary was not identified.

A CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis (Figure 2), obtained after the oral and intravenous administration of contrast material, shows the complete extent of the pelvic mass. An MRI scan of the pelvis (Figure 3) shows that the mass arises in the right adnexal region; the characteristics of the signal before and after the administration of contrast material suggest the presence of extensive regions of hemorrhage and necrosis. No normal ovarian tissue is seen on the right side. The left ovary is slightly enlarged and is displaced posteriorly by the mass.

Dr. McGee: This prepubertal girl had 18 months of recurrent abdominal pain that became acute. She had a painful, mostly solid, adnexal mass as well as fever, a moderate amount of ascites, and bilateral pleural effusions. I shall first consider the finding of the ovarian mass and then consider the other pertinent findings as a way of narrowing the diagnostic focus.

Pediatric ovarian masses are uncommon. It has been estimated that the incidence of pediatric ovarian lesions is 2.6 per 100,000 girls per year.1 In the workup of recurrent abdominal pain in girls, one must remember that the ovaries are found in the abdomen because they do not descend into the pelvis until puberty. Physical examination and an ultrasonographic study should be performed to evaluate the adnexa for masses. Although the majority of ovarian masses in girls are benign neoplasms or functional cysts, 10 to 30 percent are found to be malignant.1,2

Neoplastic Ovarian Masses

Table 3 lists benign and malignant ovarian neoplasms and the tumor markers with which they are often associated.3 They are grouped according to their cell of origin and in order of decreasing frequency of occurrence in children. It is, of course, preferable to identify the type of ovarian mass before operating, but in the majority of cases this is simply not possible. Several indicators — for instance, the radiologic appearance of the mass, laboratory values, and associated symptoms — can help suggest a diagnosis. Solid masses, such as the one in the patient under discussion, are more likely to be malignant than those that are predominantly cystic. The physical examination, with particular reference to indicators of puberty according to the stages described by Tanner,4 is diagnostically important because several of the tumors can cause isosexual precocity or masculinization. Information on tumor markers may not be available when a patient presents with acute abdominal pain but should be considered in cases that are not urgent. This patient had no evidence of masculinization or precocious puberty, suggesting that the underlying mass was not hormonally active. No neoplasm would be expected to grow as rapidly as this mass; thus, if it is a neoplasm, there must be additional, complicating factors. The possibility of malignancy remains a concern, and tissue examination remains the gold standard for diagnosis.

Non-Neoplastic Ovarian Masses

Some non-neoplastic ovarian processes may masquerade as neoplasms. Patients with cystic lesions (such as follicular cysts, cysts of the corpus luteum, the lesions of the polycystic ovarian syndrome, and endometriosis) may present with a mass, which is found on examination. Because they are more common after menarche than during early childhood and usually have a cystic appearance on imaging, such cystic lesions are unlikely in this case.

Some infections can be manifested, however uncommonly, as adnexal masses. Fungal infections, tuberculosis, parasitic infections, and pelvic inflammatory disease are all entities to keep in mind in the differential diagnosis.1 In this patient, with no exposures noted in her history, a slightly elevated neutrophil count, a slowly rising temperature, and a mass that did not have the usual hallmarks of an abscess, adnexal enlargement secondary to infection is unlikely.

There are two other non-neoplastic disorders that can produce a rapidly enlarging adnexal mass in a prepubertal girl: adnexal torsion and massive ovarian edema.5 I think we need to look closely at these entities. In the patient under discussion, the MRI scan suggests the presence of torsion in the pelvic mass.

Adnexal Torsion

Ovarian torsion is an uncommon cause of abdominal pain in children. However, in several series of children with ovarian masses, torsion was at least a component of the final diagnosis in 5 to 17 percent of the cases.6,7,8 Torsion of the ovary initially interferes with the venous and lymphatic circulation, so that there is sudden growth of the adnexa because of congestion and edema. If the torsion is unrelieved, the arterial supply may eventually be compromised — a condition that can lead to ovarian necrosis and peritonitis, mandating early surgical intervention.9 Patients present with abdominal pain that has classically been described as intense and intermittent; it may be a dull, constant pain or an intermittent, sharp pain. The pain is believed to result from the vascular occlusion. The only other consistent finding is a pelvic mass. There may be nausea and vomiting, mild leukocytosis, fever, or ileus, all of which were seen in this patient; however, none of these findings are pathognomonic of adnexal torsion.10 Because of its nonspecific manifestations, torsion has been confused with other conditions, such as appendicitis. Many patients have symptoms for days before intervention.

Ultrasonography is often used as a primary method of evaluating the pelvis in these cases. The pelvic mass associated with torsion may be solid, cystic, or complex. The only other specific sonographic sign of torsion is the presence of multiple follicles, 8 to 12 mm in size, in the cortical portion of the enlarged ovary.11 In one study,11 the finding of these follicles in the setting of diffuse swelling of the ovarian parenchyma yielded a positive predictive value of 87.5 percent, with a specificity of 93 percent, for a diagnosis of torsion. It is thought that the follicular enlargement results from the movement of fluid into the follicles, which occurs because of the congestion due to the vascular occlusion. Fluid is often seen in the cul-de-sac, but usually late in the process of venous congestion and arterial occlusion. In the patient in the current case, follicles were noted in the periphery of the mass on ultrasound and MRI examination. Doppler imaging may be used to evaluate blood flow to the organ and thus the viability of the ovary.9 In this patient, it was believed that the mass, though not very vascular, still had blood flowing through some vessels.

The most common cause of adnexal torsion is a benign cystic ovarian tumor.8,12 Malignant neoplasms, in contrast, usually cause inflammation and adhesions, making the ovary less likely to undergo torsion because it becomes adherent to surrounding structures. However, half the cases of torsion are not associated with an ovarian neoplasm; in fact, in 20 percent of the cases in one series there was no ovarian lesion at all.12 Torsion of otherwise normal adnexa is more common in premenarchal girls than it is in adults. There are a variety of theories as to how torsion of normal adnexa can happen, including the presence of congenitally long supportive ligaments, tortuosity or elongation of the mesosalpingeal vessels, tubal spasm, or abrupt changes in intraabdominal pressure, such as those that occur with vomiting, coughing, or straining due to constipation.9 Ten percent of the girls in one series had intermittent torsion characterized by repeated attacks separated by long asymptomatic intervals.12 This repetitive pattern sounds very much like the cadence of this patient's symptoms before the current admission.

Radiologic imaging studies performed on admission revealed ascites, which occasionally are seen in cases of torsion, and bilateral pleural effusions, which usually are not. The presence of ascites and pleural effusions in a patient with a solid pelvic mass frequently raises the specter of cancer, despite the relative infrequency of torsion in association with malignant tumors. The triad of hydrothorax, ascites, and a benign ovarian mass is known as Meigs' syndrome.

Meigs' Syndrome

In 1954, Joe V. Meigs, then chief of the Gynecological Service at Massachusetts General Hospital, reviewed 122 cases of abdominal or pelvic lesions associated with ascites and hydrothorax and found 84 cases of what became known as Meigs' syndrome.13 He described the syndrome as featuring a benign, solid ovarian tumor with the gross appearance of a fibroma (e.g., an actual fibroma, a thecoma, or a granulosa-cell tumor) accompanied by ascites and hydrothorax, in which removal of the tumor resulted in cure. This syndrome occurs in association with only 1 to 2 percent of fibromas, which are themselves rare in children.13 Since the original description by Meigs, ascites and hydrothorax have been reported in a variety of other gynecologic conditions including fibroids,14 degenerative ovarian changes, mature cystic teratomas, and ovarian-hyperstimulation syndrome. These cases are usually called "pseudo-Meigs' syndrome."

The mechanism of formation of the ascites is unknown. Meigs believed that it was due to leakage from a fibroma, since fibromas are frequently edematous, perhaps because of pressure on the lymphatic vessels on the tumor's surface. It has also been suggested that the presence of fluid results from cyst formation within the tumor as a result of injury or necrosis. Meigs showed that the hydrothorax generally develops from movement of the ascitic fluid into the pleural space through congenital defects in the diaphragm.

MRI and CT scans in this patient showed no evidence of metastatic disease involving the lymph nodes, bone, or abdominal organs. The absence of evidence of advanced disease and the relative rapidity with which this pelvic mass developed suggests that her symptoms were not the result of a malignant tumor. The presence of both torsion and Meigs' syndrome also make the possibility of a malignant tumor unlikely. Fibromas are rare in children, accounting for fewer than 5 percent of ovarian tumors in those 17 years of age or younger. The mass does not appear to have the imaging characteristics of a mature teratoma (i.e., a dermoid cyst), which is the most common lesion associated with pseudo-Meigs' syndrome.

Massive Ovarian Edema

In an attempt to find a unifying diagnosis for the combination of torsion of a solid ovarian mass and Meigs' syndrome in a premenarchal girl, I came across a rare non-neoplastic entity called massive ovarian edema. It was first described by Kalstone et al.15 in 1969 as a massive, solid enlargement of the ovary associated with interstitial edema, without neoplastic changes. It is generally seen in young, nulliparous women; the average age at diagnosis is 20 years, although several cases have been described in premenarchal girls. The clinical presentation ranged from an incidental finding at laparotomy to an acute abdomen; in most patients, abdominal pain and a pelvic mass were the initial findings. In women, there were occasional menstrual abnormalities and infertility. Virilization was seen in some patients. Ultrasound examination usually suggests that the tumor is solid.16 At laparotomy, one ovary, usually the right, is enlarged, anywhere from 5 cm to 40 cm in maximal dimension. It has been described as having the gross appearance of a large, grayish, and soft but solid ovary, with or without obvious torsion.

The cause of massive ovarian edema is not known, but Kalstone et al.15 and the authors of subsequent reports17,18 noted that partial or complete torsion of the mesovarium with associated venous and lymphatic obstruction was noted in a majority of the cases. In this patient with recurrent pain in the final month before admission, the idea of intermittent torsion leading to the development of massive ovarian edema seems plausible.

The approach to the treatment of massive ovarian edema is controversial. Kalstone et al.15 suggested that if intraoperative examination of a frozen section confirmed the diagnosis of massive edema, securing the ovaries to prevent torsion and waiting for regression of the edema was preferable to removing ovaries that may have the potential to function normally. Another group16 suggested that this strategy should be used only when the contralateral ovary has been compromised and that otherwise oophorectomy is the procedure of choice, since an underlying neoplasm could not be ruled out except by examination of a permanent section. However, in that study, 80 percent of the cases showed no signs of a neoplasm.

In our patient, there is the additional question of the appropriate management of advanced torsion, since the MRI scan shows possible areas of hemorrhage in the mass that suggest the presence of infarction. In advanced torsion, if the ovary appears ischemic, it is usually deemed nonviable; there has also been concern that thrombi from the pedicle of the ovary could result in embolic complications; thus, the ovary is usually removed.9 Recently, there has been an attempt to avoid removing the ovaries of girls or women who desire fertility. Two studies19,20 suggest that in most cases ovarian torsion can be reversed laparoscopically and that the ovaries remain viable after the procedure; thromboembolic events have not been observed. Sequential torsion of the contralateral ovary can occur; therefore, some have recommended fixation of the contralateral ovary as well.

In summary, I believe that the most likely diagnosis in this case is massive ovarian edema with torsion. Exploratory laparotomy with either resection of the mass or intraoperative examination of a frozen section and reduction of the torsion, depending on the findings, would be my procedure of choice.

Clinical Diagnosis (From the Medical Record)

Ovarian tumor with torsion.

Dr. Dawn M. McGee's Diagnoses

Acute and chronic adnexal torsion.

Massive ovarian edema.

Pathological Discussion

Dr. Robert H. Young: The diagnostic procedure was exploratory laparotomy with removal of the right ovary and fallopian tube. The uterus and the left ovary and fallopian tube were normal on intraoperative inspection. The ovarian mass weighed 390 g and was 14.0 by 9.5 by 5.5 cm in overall dimension. The outer surface of the mass was smooth and glistening. Sectioning revealed an edematous parenchyma with striking areas of hemorrhage (Figure 4). Microscopical examination confirmed the presence of hemorrhage, but of greater interest was the presence of marked edema in the stroma; pale spindle cells with bland cytologic features were separated by pale staining tissue (Figure 5). This edematous tissue surrounded residual follicular derivatives of the underlying ovarian tissue. The morphologic features were those of massive edema and areas of hemorrhage consistent with the presence of torsion, confirming Dr. McGee's clinical impression.5

Massive edema is distinguished from an edematous ovarian fibroma by the pattern of the edema, which surrounds the underlying ovarian structures. In a fibroma, the abnormal tissue largely effaces the underlying normal structures. Several other tumor-like lesions of the ovary may form solid masses that can easily be considered neoplasms on gross inspection. One of these, which is probably closely related to massive edema, is ovarian fibromatosis. This lesion, which bears no relation to the soft-tissue tumor of the same name, surrounds rather than effaces the underlying normal ovarian structures, as does massive edema. In contrast to massive edema, however, it has a firm, white, fibrous character simulating a conventional solid fibroma, rather than an edematous fibroma. Occasionally, one sees edema in a lesion that is predominantly fibromatosis or some fibromatous areas in a lesion that consists predominantly of massive edema, suggesting that, at least in some cases, there may be a relation between these two intriguing non-neoplastic lesions.21 Fibromatosis, like massive edema, has been seen in children. In contrast, most other solid tumor-like lesions of the ovary5 are usually seen in the reproductive or postmenopausal age groups.

The important clinical message is that this is a benign, non-neoplastic condition. If the diagnosis is made on intraoperative examination, biopsy of the contralateral ovary or omentectomy is not needed.

Dr. Robert E. Scully (Pathology): If this patient came back with left-lower-quadrant pain very similar to the right-lower-quadrant pain, would you explore the abdomen at that time and possibly tack down the opposite ovary to the pelvic wall?

Dr. McGee: If careful evaluation did not reveal any other cause of the pain, it would be reasonable to consider exploration by laparoscopy. If no other abnormalities were found, I would tack her ovary to a stable structure to prevent any future torsion. It has been suggested that this procedure be carried out at the time of the first operation for torsion of a normal ovary. There has, however, been concern about the theoretical possibility of compromised blood flow to the ovary and a resulting decrease in fertility as a consequence of this procedure.

Dr. Nancy Lee Harris: The patient's primary care physician reports that the patient is doing well and has had no recurrence of abdominal pain. Her diabetes is being managed by an endocrine consultant.

Massive edema of the ovary with torsion.
