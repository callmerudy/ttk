Case 22-2002 — A 37-Year-Old Man with Unexplained Fever after a Long Trip through South America
James T. Noble, and Eugene J. Mark

Presentation of Case

A 37-year-old man was admitted to the hospital because of three weeks of intermittent fever and abdominal and back pain.

The patient had been well until about seven months earlier, one month after he had begun a difficult trip by land from his native Peru to the United States. During his trip, which took four months, he ate fruit directly from trees, bathed in and drank water from rivers and other uncertain sources, passed through several tropical rain forests, and received numerous mosquito bites. In Guatemala, about 7 months before admission, he had a fever that lasted 10 days before subsiding without treatment. During the ensuing month, he had fever and watery, nonbloody diarrhea for four days. In Mexico, five months before admission, he drank milk but ate no cheese. He again had diarrhea, which lasted 1 week, and fever, which lasted 11 days; he received a 1-week course of an unidentified antibiotic. During the following two months, he traveled by car from Mexico to Boston, arriving three months before admission. At that time he was seen at another facility because of dysuria; there, he was told that he had anemia and was given an antibiotic for a "kidney infection." He was also told that a tuberculin skin test was negative and was advised that the results of liver-function tests were abnormal. However, he felt better after treatment. He reported being free of fever for an uncertain interval thereafter.

About six weeks before admission, "burning" epigastric pain, without radiation, developed; despite the pain his appetite remained intact and his activities and diet remained unchanged. Three weeks before admission, the pain shifted to the left upper quadrant and left shoulder, increased in severity, radiated to the left upper portion of his back, and was aggravated by bending or lifting. At about the same time, he began to have intermittent episodes of fever, accompanied by profuse night sweats and rigors, and he passed black stools for several days. Ten days before admission, he saw fresh blood in the stool once. About six days before admission, the fever recurred. He went to the same facility and was given amoxicillin and an antacid to take for two days. He did not return for follow-up and finally came to this hospital.

The patient had worked as a carpenter. He had a 12-year monogamous relationship with his wife, who remained in Peru, and he reported having had no sexual exposures since leaving her. He had lost about 8 kg in weight during recent months. There was no history of exposure to animals or ill persons and no history of viral hepatitis, jaundice, arthralgia, anorexia, vomiting, hematemesis, constipation, productive cough, dyspnea, or hematuria. He did not use alcohol, tobacco, or illicit drugs, and had not eaten uncooked seafood or meat. His mother had had tuberculosis about 15 years earlier.

The temperature was 40.0°C, the pulse was 90, and the respirations were 18. The blood pressure was 120/80 mm Hg. The oxygen saturation was 96 percent while the patient was breathing ambient air.

On physical examination, the patient appeared fatigued but not acutely ill. No rash, skin lesions, or lymphadenopathy was found. The head and neck were normal and the lungs clear. A grade 2 precordial systolic murmur did not radiate. The abdomen was soft, with tenderness but no guarding, on deep palpation of the left upper quadrant; the liver and spleen were not felt, and there was no costovertebral tenderness. The arms and legs were normal, and rectal examination revealed no abnormalities; a stool specimen was negative for occult blood. Neurologic examination was unremarkable. Radiographs of the chest showed that the lungs were clear and that the size of the heart was normal. Minimal loss of height and minor degenerative changes were observed in one midthoracic vertebral body. A computed tomographic (CT) scan of the abdomen and pelvis revealed two foci of low attenuation, both smaller than 1.0 cm in diameter, in the spleen. There was also an area of low attenuation, 0.9 cm in diameter, in the midpole of the right kidney; it was suspected to be a small cyst. Repeated CT examination of the abdomen and pelvis revealed that the two hypodense splenic lesions were slightly smaller than they had been on the previous examination. A CT scan of the thorax showed no abnormalities. A transthoracic cardiac ultrasonographic study revealed no Doppler evidence of mitral-valve prolapse or mitral regurgitation; there was only trace tricuspid and pulmonic regurgitation.

Staining of a stool specimen disclosed abundant mixed bacteria and no neutrophils; a culture grew no organisms. Examination of the stool for ova and parasites revealed Dientamoeba fragilis trophozoites in the first of two specimens and no helminthic ova. Thick and thin, Giemsa-stained peripheral-blood smears, including a smear of a specimen of blood obtained during a bout of fever, showed no malarial or babesial microorganisms.

Specimens of blood and urine were obtained for culture, and intravenous administration of vancomycin and gentamicin was begun. The temperature was normal during part of nearly every day but peaked at 38.5°C to 39.1°C during the first three days, 37.6°C on the fourth day, and 37.9°C to 38.4°C on the fifth and sixth days. On the third hospital day, fiberoptic endoscopic examination to the second part of the duodenum revealed that the esophagus and stomach were normal. The duodenal mucosa was diffusely congested, with mild edema and no active bleeding. A duodenal-fluid aspirate contained no parasites, and a biopsy specimen revealed superficial fragments of duodenal mucosa with a moderate monocytic infiltrate of the lamina propria and increased lymphocytic exocytosis. Colonoscopic examination revealed no abnormalities. By the fourth hospital day, the patient felt considerably better. All the culture specimens (six blood cultures, one fungal blood culture, and a urine culture) were sterile. On the sixth hospital day, while he was taking only ferrous sulfate, the patient was discharged, with arrangements for follow-up as an outpatient.

One week after discharge, the patient came to the infectious-disease clinic and reported having had daily fevers, with a temperature as high as 39.4°C; the pain in the left upper quadrant and back persisted. On physical examination, the temperature was 39.0°C, the pulse 100, and the blood pressure 120/75 mm Hg. The patient appeared flushed. No cutaneous lesion or lymphadenopathy was detected. The lungs were clear, and a grade 2 systolic precordial murmur was heard. The abdomen was normal except for minimal tenderness in the epigastrium and left upper quadrant. No synovitis or genital or rectal lesions were detected. The results of laboratory tests are shown in Table 1 and Table 2. A CT scan of the thorax, abdomen, and pelvis showed no lymphadenopathy in the thorax. The two splenic lesions had continued to decrease in size and were barely perceptible. A simple cyst was again seen in the right kidney. Another set of thick and thin Giemsa-stained smears showed no organisms. Additional urine and blood specimens were obtained for culture.

Two weeks later, the patient reported that he had remained afebrile for one week but subsequently had had daily temperature spikes ranging from 38.3°C to 39.2°C, with nightly shaking chills and sweats. Pain in the left upper abdominal quadrant was intermittently present but had diminished. There was no nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea. He continued to take only ferrous sulfate.

The temperature was 38.6°C. No rash was seen. A lymph node, 1.0 cm in diameter, was present in the left supraclavicular fossa; a smaller one was present in the left axilla. Two blood cultures, a mycobacterial urine culture, and an additional set of thick and thin Giemsa-stained smears for malaria with specimens from the previous visit were negative. The results of laboratory tests are shown in Table 1 and Table 2.

A diagnostic procedure was performed.

Differential Diagnosis

Dr. James T. Noble*: The differential diagnosis of fever, anemia, and nonspecific constitutional symptoms is immense and includes neoplasms, immunologic and idiopathic granulomatous diseases, and infections (Table 4). In this case, we must take into account the patient's overland journey from Peru to the United States, during which time he may have been exposed to many infectious agents that can account for protracted fever. The abnormalities in the spleen, the slightly abnormal aminotransferase levels, the progressive anemia, and the evidence of blood loss from the small bowel suggest an infectious or neoplastic process involving cells of the lymphoid, monocyte, or macrophage lineage, thus somewhat narrowing the differential diagnosis. In addition, the several short courses of antimicrobial therapy that were given resulted in transient improvement.

Neoplasms

Lymphomas are usually associated with palpable or radiologically evident lymphadenopathy or hepatosplenomegaly, neither of which was present in this case. However, patients with Hodgkin's lymphoma or non-Hodgkin's lymphoma may present with fever and involvement of only the spleen, bowel, or bone marrow. A seven-month illness without evidence of regional lymph-node involvement or other disease would be unusual in a patient with intestinal lymphoma.

Protozoal and Parasitic Diseases

Malaria, caused by either Plasmodium vivax or P. ovale, must always be considered in a patient who is returning from an area where malaria is endemic and who presents with fever and nonlocalizing symptoms. This patient may have been exposed to plasmodium organisms through mosquito bites, and he had anemia with evidence of splenic inflammation. However, multiple smears for malaria were negative, and he did not have the typical malarial paroxysms of fever.

Patients with visceral leishmaniasis, or kala-azar, may present with protracted, nonlocalizing fever, but as the disease progresses and amastigotes proliferate within macrophages, hepatosplenomegaly usually develops.1 Fever due to leishmania infection would not be expected to respond to antimicrobial therapy.

Patients with schistosomiasis may present with fever of unknown origin and minimal or no localizing signs. In chronic schistosomiasis, abdominal pain, elevated hepatic aminotransferase levels, and gastrointestinal blood loss are frequent findings.2 A history of exposure to fresh water in a region where Schistosoma mansoni infection is endemic should prompt consideration of this diagnosis. However, chronic schistosomiasis is associated with heavy excretion of eggs in the stool,3 and in the current case there were no parasitic ova in the stool.

Infection with Strongyloides stercoralis tends to localize in the upper small bowel; patients may present with weight loss, abdominal pain, and occult (or occasionally overt) gastrointestinal blood loss.4 In the strongyloides hyperinfection syndrome, rhabditiform larvae within the intestine develop into infective filariform larvae, with subsequent migration into the lymphatic vessels and bloodstream. This syndrome may cause repeated episodes of sepsis4 and thus is a component of the differential diagnosis of fever and chills with duodenitis. However, patients with the strongyloides hyperinfection syndrome typically have eosinophilia, and organisms are easily seen in duodenal aspirates and biopsy specimens.

Rickettsial Infection

Infective endocarditis is frequently considered in the differential diagnosis of prolonged fever without localizing symptoms.2,5 Although this patient had a cardiac murmur, there were no abnormal findings on blood cultures or on an echocardiogram. Q fever, a rickettsial disease caused by infection with Coxiella burnetii, may cause culture-negative endocarditis,6 but such endocarditis is typically associated with large vegetations and the destruction of valve tissue.7 In addition, C. burnetii is usually transmitted to humans by contact with infected animal material, especially placental tissue, and there is no history of such contact in this case.

Fungal Infection

Various fungal pathogens may cause a syndrome of protracted fever with nonspecific constitutional manifestations. According to this patient's history, possible diagnoses include histoplasmosis,8 blastomycosis,9 paracoccidioidomycosis,10 and perhaps coccidioidomycosis.11 In the absence of characteristic mucocutaneous or pulmonary findings, however, these diseases could be diagnosed only with an invasive procedure and would not be expected to improve with antibacterial therapy.

Mycobacterial Infection

Tuberculosis is mentioned as an important cause of fever of unknown origin in both early12,13 and more recent5,14 reviews. This patient had a history of exposure to a person with tuberculosis (his mother) and was known to have a reactive tuberculin test, indicating that he had had infection in the past. The slight loss of height of a thoracic vertebra on his chest film may indicate healed tuberculous vertebral osteomyelitis (Pott's disease). If tuberculosis is the explanation for his condition, I would postulate that he had reactivation of a past infection, with subsequent dissemination by the reticuloendothelial system to the liver, spleen, and bone marrow. It is also possible that he was infected with Mycobacterium tuberculosis or M. bovis during his recent journey. In this circumstance, the microorganisms' portal of entry would presumably have been the gastrointestinal tract (if the patient drank contaminated milk). Could primary intestinal tuberculosis explain the findings in this case?

Intestinal tuberculosis occurs either when infected secretions are swallowed (in patients with pulmonary tuberculosis) or when contaminated dairy products are ingested.15 Many patients who acquire such infection by drinking contaminated milk or eating contaminated cheese have no abnormalities on chest radiographs.16 The disease typically involves the distal small bowel, where the highest concentration of gut-associated lymphoid tissue is found, and is usually associated with abdominal lymphadenopathy and peritonitis. Radiologic17 or endoscopic studies usually identify abnormalities such as a mass or mucosal ulceration. In the current case, the absence of a mass, of radiologic findings suggesting carcinoma or another obstructive lesion, and of abdominal lymphadenopathy makes intestinal tuberculosis unlikely.

Bacterial Infection

            Salmonellosis

Enteric fever caused by Salmonella typhi (typhoid fever), S. paratyphi A (paratyphoid fever), S. schottmuelleri, or S. hirschfeldii should always be considered in patients who have recently arrived from areas where these organisms are endemic and who present with fever, abdominal pain, slightly abnormal results on liver-function tests, and nonspecific findings on examination.18 However, symptoms of enteric fever usually resolve within four weeks.18 Relapses are uncommon (occurring in only about 10 percent of patients)19 and tend to be less severe than the initial episode. This patient's febrile symptoms have not diminished over time, which makes enteric fever an unlikely diagnosis.

            Tularemia

A prolonged febrile syndrome is one of the manifestations of infection with Francisella tularensis, or tularemia. However, fever due to tularemia usually resolves within four to five weeks,20 and this patient had a negative serologic test for tularemia, making that diagnosis unlikely.

            Brucellosis

Brucellosis is an acute and chronic infection of the reticuloendothelial system that is caused by various species of the genus brucella.21 Humans acquire brucellosis from exposure to infected animals or contaminated animal products; most cases in the United States occur in slaughterhouse workers, farmers, veterinarians, and other people who handle animals.22 In countries where infection of domestic animals is common, milk and cheese are vehicles for the organisms that cause human brucellosis — Brucella melitensis in the case of cows and B. abortus in the case of goats.21,22 Patients with acute brucellosis present with nonspecific symptoms, including fever, sweats, malaise, and anorexia.22 Persistent brucella infection may involve several organ systems, leading to ulcerations of the gastrointestinal tract,21 splenic abscess or splenomegaly, and osteoarticular complications, including osteomyelitis, arthritis, tenosynovitis, bursitis, and spondylitis. Sacroiliitis is the most frequently reported articular complication of brucellosis.23 Patients with undulant fever (one of the many synonyms for brucellosis) may have a normal temperature for days or weeks, interspersed with acute episodes of fever.22 Unlike typhoid fever, which has a similar pattern of organ involvement, untreated brucellosis can persist for months or years.

Blood cultures in cases of brucellosis often yield no organisms, and unless organisms are recovered from the blood or bone marrow, the diagnosis rests principally on serologic test results. The brucella agglutinin test measures antibodies against B. melitensis, B. suis, and B. abortus, reflecting infection acquired from cattle, swine, and goats, respectively.24 A second, specific antibody test is required to diagnose B. canis infection. In some patients with brucellosis, no antibody is detectable at low serum dilutions; this is known as the prozone phenomenon.25 It may be necessary to test serum at a dilution higher than 1:320 to detect brucella agglutinins, especially in patients with chronic disease.24 In addition, the serum of some patients with chronic brucellosis has been described as containing "blocking" antibodies (observed as an antiglobulin reaction caused by polyclonal B-cell activation).26 When the diagnosis is strongly suspected and agglutination tests for brucella are negative, a Coombs' test or a test for blocking antibodies is indicated.27

In summary, a diagnosis of chronic brucellosis with the recent onset of sacroiliitis is consistent with the findings in this case. The diagnostic procedure was probably a biopsy to obtain bone marrow for culture. Alternatively, it may have been a test of the serum at a high dilution to uncover a prozone phenomenon or an indirect Coombs' test to detect blocking antibodies — strategies that would permit a serologic diagnosis without an invasive procedure.

Pathological Discussion

Dr. Mark: The diagnostic procedure was aspiration and biopsy of the bone marrow and culture of the bone marrow specimens. Histopathologically, the biopsy specimen contained a single small granuloma (Figure 1). Stains for acid-fast bacilli and fungi were negative. Culture of the aspirate yielded B. melitensis.

Brucellosis, also known as undulant fever, is truly a geographic disease, in that early descriptions focused on the area where an epidemic developed. Thus, it has been known as Malta fever, Mediterranean fever, Gibraltar fever, and Constantinople fever. The organism usually enters humans through the gastrointestinal tract or an abrasion in the skin, although the conjunctiva is another source of entry. It enters histiocytes and is transmitted throughout the body through the bloodstream. The bacteria multiply in the reticuloendothelial cells in the spleen, liver, bone marrow, and other organs. Histopathologically, brucellosis is characterized by granulomas in lymph nodes, spleen, liver, and bone marrow.28 Bacteria are found in macrophages in many organs in experimentally infected animals and lie within the granulomas (Figure 2).29,30 The organisms are small gram-negative coccobacilli, which may stain only faintly.31

Dr. Hurtado, how did you treat this patient?

Dr. Hurtado: On the basis of a preliminary microbiologic diagnosis of brucellosis, we implemented a six-week regimen of doxycycline and rifampin. The patient's fevers remitted within one week after the start of treatment. Throughout the course of his illness, no focal manifestations of brucellosis developed. We were surprised that the slide-agglutination test for brucellosis was negative. The test was repeated at various dilutions to rule out the prozone phenomenon, but these results were also negative. Subsequently, a tube-agglutination test was carried out at the Massachusetts State Laboratory on the initial serum sample and was strongly positive at a titer of 1:1280. The patient continues to do well.

Dr. Joseph F. Arena (Neurosurgery): Dr. Noble, would you comment on the bony manifestations of brucellosis, particularly sacroiliitis?

Dr. Noble: There are two main types of osteoarticular manifestations of brucellosis: those that are caused by invasion of the bones or joints by the organism and those that are thought to be immunologic in origin. Both axial spondylitis and sacroiliitis are thought to be of immunologic origin rather than the result of direct infection. Osteomyelitis and septic arthritis are both complications of brucellosis, but septic arthritis principally affects the large peripheral joints (as is true of most other bacterial septic arthritides).

Two features of this case contradict popular teaching. First, the case raised the issues of the prozone phenomenon and the existence of brucella-blocking antibodies, both of which are thought of as legends in the study of infectious diseases, although the truth is remarkably difficult to sort out. Many authorities have questioned the existence of the prozone phenomenon, suggesting that the agglutinin test is always positive when the disease is truly present (i.e., that there are no false negative results). However, in the case under discussion the test was properly performed, and the test result was negative despite a positive diagnosis.

Second, many reviews in the literature say that brucellosis, unlike S. typhi infection, does not cause gastrointestinal bleeding, and many observers believe that the presence of blood in the stool argues against brucellosis. However, this patient had gastrointestinal bleeding.

Pathological Diagnosis

Brucella melitensis infection.
